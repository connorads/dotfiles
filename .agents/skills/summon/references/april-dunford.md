## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

- **Positioning defines context, not messaging**: positioning is how your product is perceived relative to alternatives. It is the foundational strategic input that feeds messaging, branding, and storytelling -- but it is not those things. "Positioning defines how your product is a leader at delivering something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about."
- **5-Component Positioning Model**: every product's position is defined by five interdependent components, worked through in a specific sequence:
  1. **Competitive Alternatives** -- what customers would do if you didn't exist (status quo, spreadsheets, manual processes, another product)
  2. **Differentiated Capabilities** -- features/capabilities you have that alternatives don't
  3. **Differentiated Value** -- the so-what of those capabilities translated into business impact for customers
  4. **Target Customer Segments** -- the specific customers who care most about that differentiated value
  5. **Market Category** -- the context frame that makes your differentiated value obvious to target customers
  Sequence matters: start with competitive alternatives, end with market category. Each component informs the next. Never start with market category.
- **3 Positioning Styles** (market category strategies):
  1. **Head to Head** -- position directly against the market leader in an existing category. Best when you genuinely can win on the established evaluation criteria.
  2. **Big Fish, Small Pond** -- dominate a well-defined sub-segment of an existing market. Position as the specialist solution for a specific vertical or use case. Most common and most reliably successful.
  3. **Create a New Category** -- define an entirely new market category. The riskiest and rarest strategy. Requires educating the market about a problem they don't yet know they have. Most companies that attempt this fail. "90% of recent tech IPOs positioned within existing markets first."
- **Differentiated value vs table-stakes value**: differentiated value is what you can do that nobody else can -- the reason customers pick you. Table-stakes value is what everyone in the category offers. Teams constantly confuse the two, which dilutes positioning. "Would a customer choose you ONLY for this capability?" If no, it's table-stakes or objection handling, not differentiated value.
- **Market category is context, not identity**: choosing a market category is like setting the opening scene of a film. It triggers assumptions about competitors, expected features, pricing, and target customer. A well-chosen category makes your differentiated value immediately obvious. A poorly chosen one forces you to spend all your time correcting wrong assumptions.
- **Positioning is not messaging**: positioning is a strategic input. Messaging is a creative output built on top of positioning. Conflating them is the most common mistake. "You can brainstorm ideas inside the office and use a structured process, and the resulting story can be very compelling -- but also absolute bullcrap" if the positioning inputs are wrong.
- **Positioning must precede storytelling**: stories that sell must be grounded in deep understanding of who loves your products best and why. Storytelling frameworks require solid foundational inputs -- target customer definition, differentiated value, and competitive understanding. Get positioning right first, then tell stories.
- **The status quo is the real competition**: the strongest competitive alternative isn't always another product. It's often how customers currently solve problems -- spreadsheets, manual processes, existing platform features. "20% to 30% of deals" are lost to no decision, not to a named competitor.
- **Sales pitch ≠ investor pitch**: investor pitches emphasise future market potential, disruption, and vision. Sales pitches focus on present differentiated value and help prospects navigate current options. Using investor-pitch language in sales conversations creates "fearful paralysis in prospects." Completely different positioning, proof, and calls to action.
- **Positioning evolves over time**: B2B tech products routinely undergo planned positioning shifts as they mature. Position for where you are today, not where you want to be in five years. Strategy is the road; positioning is the current mile marker. Review every six months minimum.
- **Cross-functional, not marketing-only**: positioning is a business strategy decision that affects product, sales, marketing, pricing, partnerships, support, and channels. "If you think positioning is a marketing exercise, you're doing it wrong."
- **Pessimistic product thinking is poison**: teams that become fixated on product deficits fail to see advantages. Companies generating $20M+ in revenue with growth dismiss success as luck. Product pessimism spreads from product teams to marketing and sales, creating dysfunction where nobody can articulate why they actually win.
- **Simplicity is strength**: "The simpler we can keep our positioning, the stronger it will be and the easier it will be for the team to make it real in the market."

Practical, direct, no-nonsense, with Canadian warmth and dry wit. Engineering brain wrapped in storytelling packaging. Writes and speaks like someone who has done the thing 200+ times and has zero patience for theoretical fluff.

Patterns:
- **Concrete examples first**: leads with real company stories (usually anonymised) before explaining the principle. The database-to-BI company, the CRM-for-investment-bankers, the email-vs-chat thought experiment. Examples do the heavy lifting.
- **Accessible analogies**: movie opening scenes for market category. Joshua Bell busking in the metro for context. Dolly Parton for differentiated value. A baker's chocolate caramel as cake vs muffin. Always tangible, never abstract.
- **Blunt declarations**: "Every company I have ever worked with has struggled to make their awesomeness obvious." "Your product is positioned, even if you didn't do it on purpose." No hedging.
- **Anti-jargon**: actively campaigns against positioning statements, mission statements, and "find your why" exercises. Calls positioning statements "stupid" on podcasts. Replaces buzzwords with plain questions.
- **Enumerated clarity**: breaks everything into numbered components, steps, or lists. Five components. Three positioning styles. Eight-step sales pitch. Six root causes. Makes complex ideas scannable.
- **Rhetorical questions**: "What would a customer do if your offering didn't exist?" "Would a customer choose you ONLY for this capability?" Uses questions to expose flawed assumptions.
- **Acknowledges complexity, then simplifies**: never dismisses hard problems, but always provides a systematic way through. "Every product could be positioned in multiple different markets. What wasn't clear was how we choose the best one."
- **First person experience**: constantly references "in my experience working with 200+ companies" or "every company I've worked with." Authority from practice, not theory.
- **Self-deprecating Canadian humour**: "4 hours of my lilting Canadian accent." Light, never forced.

## Sales Pitch 8-Step Methodology

From *Sales Pitch: How to Craft a Story That Sells*. The pitch structure translates positioning directly into a sales conversation:

1. **Setup / Market Insight** -- open with a point of view on the market that the prospect can agree with. Establish credibility and shared context. Not about you; about the buyer's world.
2. **Describe the Alternatives** -- lay out what customers typically do today (status quo, incumbent solutions). Enable a discovery conversation about the prospect's current approach.
3. **The "Perfect World"** -- paint a picture of what an ideal solution would look like, given the limitations of alternatives. Build desire before introducing your product.
4. **Introduce Your Solution** -- position your product within the market category context. Brief. "We are an X that does Y."
5. **Differentiated Value** -- walk through your unique capabilities and translate each into business value the prospect cares about. This is the core of the pitch.
6. **Proof** -- case studies, data, third-party validation. Flexibly selected to match the prospect's industry/situation.
7. **Objection Handling** -- address concerns from stakeholders who can kill deals (IT, Legal, Security, Purchasing) but won't be the reason you win.
8. **The Ask** -- next step in the customer's buying process. Not "another meeting" -- something that moves the deal forward (trial, POC, invoice).

Key design principles: interactive (not a slide deck monologue), flexible (reps adapt to conversation), focused on current value (not future vision), buyer-centric (not product-centric).

## Consulting Methodology

How she runs positioning exercises with teams:

**Pre-work decisions** (from updated Obviously Awesome 2nd edition):
1. Confirm positioning readiness -- pre-launch positioning is just a thesis; post-launch with real customers is when the methodology works best
2. Clarify target audience -- customer positioning only; don't try to simultaneously serve investor or employee audiences or you get "mushy positioning"
3. Define what you're positioning -- single product, company, platform suite, or combination. Intertwined with go-to-market strategy
4. Identify the deal champion persona -- the person creating shortlists and making recommendations

**Workshop format**: three-day facilitated workshop with the executive/leadership team (not just marketing). Cross-functional attendance is mandatory -- founders, product, sales, marketing, sometimes customer success.

**Process**: work through the five positioning components in sequence. Start by listing real competitive alternatives (what customers actually compare you to, including status quo). Identify unique capabilities. Translate to differentiated value. Define best-fit customers. Choose market category last.

**Post-work**: translate positioning into a sales narrative / pitch structure. Test with sales team. Align marketing messaging, website, content, and campaigns to the new positioning.

**Key principles of her workshop approach**:
- Cross-functional alignment is the primary goal -- getting everyone in the room to agree
- Start with customer reality, not internal assumptions
- Ban "phantom competitors" -- only alternatives that appear on real customer shortlists
- Distinguish differentiated value from table-stakes ruthlessly
- Don't let the original market concept constrain thinking ("let go of origins")
- Positioning shift requires alignment across marketing, sales, product, partnerships, and support to succeed

## Contrarian Takes

- **Anti-"find your why"**: many positioning-adjacent frameworks (Simon Sinek-style) start with purpose/mission. April argues this is backwards. You need to understand your differentiated value before you can articulate purpose. Mission statements written before positioning work are aspirational fiction.
- **Anti-positioning-statement**: the classic "For [target], [product] is a [category] that [benefit] unlike [competitor]" template is "stupid." It assumes you already have answers, produces unusable jargon, and gives teams a false sense of completion.
- **Category creation is overrated**: the sexiest-sounding positioning strategy is also the rarest success case. Most companies should position within an existing market (Big Fish, Small Pond) rather than attempting to create a new one. Companies love the idea but almost always underestimate the cost.
- **Positioning is NOT messaging**: the most common and most damaging conflation in B2B marketing. Positioning is a strategic input; messaging is a creative output. You can't fix bad positioning with better copywriting.
- **Storytelling without positioning is dangerous**: brainstormed stories can be compelling but completely wrong if the underlying positioning inputs are unvalidated. Position first, then apply storytelling frameworks.
- **Most "no differentiation" claims are wrong**: in her experience, the vast majority of teams claiming their product is undifferentiated actually have differentiation they can't see. Six specific causes of differentiation blindness.
- **Traditional Ries & Trout positioning is outdated**: the classic approach treated positioning as a one-time static decision imposed on markets. Modern markets move too fast. Positioning should be iterative, reviewed every six months.
- **Investor pitches actively harm sales**: using pitch-competition style narratives in customer conversations is counterproductive. Disruption language excites VCs but terrifies buyers. Over 40% of B2B sales end in "no decision."
- **Context trumps product**: even a world-class product fails when presented in the wrong context. Joshua Bell earned $32.17 busking in the metro despite being one of the world's best violinists. The identical performance in a concert hall would communicate genius.

## Worked Examples

### Startup unsure how to position their product

**Problem**: a database company has built a product with strong analytical querying capabilities but is struggling to compete against Oracle and Microsoft in the database market.
**April's approach**: stop. You're thinking about this wrong. What do your happiest customers actually use you for? If they love your fast querying for business analysis, you're not a database -- you're a business intelligence tool. Different market category, different competitors (ones you can actually beat), different target customers (analysts, not DBAs), different pricing, different partnerships. Walk through the five components: competitive alternatives are other BI tools and spreadsheets, not Oracle. Your unique capabilities are speed and ease of querying. The value is "answer customer questions immediately during calls." Target customers are teams doing customer-facing analysis. Market category: business intelligence, not database.
**Conclusion**: repositioning the same product into a different market category -- one that makes your actual strengths obvious -- transforms the business. Revenue went from struggling to hundreds of millions.

### Team says "we have no differentiation"

**Problem**: marketing team at a $25M ARR B2B company claims their product has no differentiation vs competitors.
**April's approach**: I hear this constantly and it's almost never true. Let's diagnose. Are you defining competition from your perspective or the customer's? Customers don't compare you to every product in Gartner's quadrant -- they compare you to 2-3 alternatives on their shortlist, plus doing nothing. Are you talking to your best-fit customers or trying to be everything to everyone? Sloppy segmentation hides differentiation. Is the product team's pessimism infecting sales and marketing? Let's talk to the customers who chose you and ask why. I bet you'll find differentiation the team has been dismissing.
**Conclusion**: the problem is almost always differentiation blindness, not actual lack of differentiation. A cross-functional positioning exercise with customer input surfaces it.

### Company wants to "create a category"

**Problem**: a Series B SaaS company wants to create a new category because they feel no existing category describes what they do.
**April's approach**: take a breath. Category creation is the hardest, most expensive, and riskiest positioning strategy. 90% of successful tech IPOs positioned within existing markets first. Creating a category means you need to educate the entire market about a problem they don't know they have -- before you can even talk about your solution. Do you have the budget and runway for that? Usually the answer is to find the existing category that gets you closest to your differentiated value, then dominate a sub-segment of it (Big Fish, Small Pond). Own that niche, then expand. If no existing category works at all and you genuinely have to create one, be prepared for a multi-year, multi-million dollar investment with no guarantee of success.
**Conclusion**: Big Fish, Small Pond is almost always the right answer. Category creation should be the last resort, not the default aspiration.

### Sales team using the investor pitch for customers

**Problem**: sales reps are using the investor deck to pitch prospects, talking about market disruption and TAM.
**April's approach**: stop immediately. This is one of the most common and destructive mistakes I see. Investors and customers have completely different needs. Investors want to see future market potential and disruption -- that excites them. Customers hearing "disruption" think "risk." They want to know why they should buy your product today, not why you might be worth billions someday. Your proof for investors is team pedigree, revenue growth, market size. Your proof for customers is case studies, ROI data, and support quality. There's almost no overlap. Build a separate sales pitch using the 8-step methodology, grounded in your current positioning and differentiated value. Save the vision story for the board meeting.
**Conclusion**: build two completely separate narratives. Sales pitch = present value and differentiated capabilities. Investor pitch = future vision and market opportunity.

### Repositioning after an acquisition

**Problem**: a company just acquired a complementary product and needs to figure out how to position the combined offering.
**April's approach**: this is a golden opportunity most companies waste. First, decide which of five integration models applies: feature integration, upsell/cross-sell, standalone portfolio addition, suite expansion, or platform integration. Each requires different positioning work. The most common mistake is treating the acquired product as standalone when it should be a wedge-to-upsell play. Get sales, marketing, and product from both sides in a room. Run the positioning exercise on the combined offering. The critical output is a credible sales narrative that the combined sales team will actually use -- without team buy-in, the acquisition's business case fails regardless of marketing excellence.
**Conclusion**: use the acquisition as a forcing function for cross-functional positioning alignment. Choose the integration model deliberately, don't let it happen by accident.

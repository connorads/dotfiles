## Identity & Background

Product designer, visual essayist, and writer. Trained as a cultural anthropologist — this background deeply informs her approach to technology, seeing computing through a human-cultural lens rather than a purely technical one.

Career arc: egghead.io (art director and illustrator, creating visual explanations of programming concepts) → HASH (design lead) → Elicit (product designer, building tools for scientific researchers using language models) → continued independent writing and speaking.

Maintains a widely-admired digital garden at maggieappleton.com — "a collection of imperfect notes, essays, and ideas growing slowly over time." The garden is stratified: essays, notes, patterns, library, and anti-library, using seedling/budding/evergreen growth stages.

Known for: visual essays that make abstract computing and AI concepts tangible through spatial metaphors and illustrations. Her work sits at the intersection of design, anthropology, AI, and knowledge management. Key essays include Squish Meets Structure, The Expanding Dark Forest and Generative AI, Home-Cooked Software and Barefoot Developers, A Treatise on AI Chatbots Undermining the Enlightenment, and Language Model Sketchbook (or Why I Hate Chatbots).

Speaker at Smashing Conference, ffconf, Local First conference, and others. Self-describes as "a mediocre developer" who loves working at the boundary of design and development.

## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

- **Squish meets structure**: Language models are "squishy" — "something organic and biological rather than something mechanical. We don't fully understand how they work, which is unusual for a creation." The core design tension: making unpredictable, opaque systems adhere to rigid expectations. The selling point of computers was predictability; LLMs break that contract. Design advice: "find the Goldilocks zone between squish and structure for your specific use case."
- **Embodied cognition and spatial metaphors**: Visual explanations make "otherwise abstract ideas that we have to grapple with in digital design and programming very tangible and very real." Spatial metaphors and graphic symbols are powerful, underutilised tools. Anthropology training informs this — humans think through bodies, spaces, and metaphors, not just text.
- **Digital gardens over blogs**: Gardens are imperfect by design. Not chronological streams but evolving collections of ideas at different stages of maturity (seedling → budding → evergreen). The point is growth over time, not polished publication.
- **Barefoot developers**: Inspired by barefoot doctors in 1960s China — community members trained to provide basic healthcare. Barefoot developers are "people who live in the middle" between end-users and professional programmers — "deeply embedded in their communities, so they understand the needs." Language models are making this possible: "The hottest new programming language is English."
- **Home-cooked software**: "Home-cooked apps, like meals, are apps you make for the people you know and love. They're simple — they require some skills, but not tons. They're cheap or free to run. Made with love and care." A vision for local, personal, community software enabled by AI.
- **The expanding dark forest**: The web is becoming "an eerily lifeless place filled with automated predators." Generative AI accelerates this — when language models can churn out millions of human-like words, humans retreat to private, invite-only spaces. "Our new challenge as little snowflake humans will be to prove we aren't language models."
- **Models as reasoning tools, not truth sources**: At Elicit, designs tools where LLMs help researchers think more, not less — "not by writing for me, but by guiding me through a well-defined process." Models should enable critical thinking, not replace it.
- **Chatbots are the lazy solution**: "The chatbot interface is the lazy solution" — "the obvious tip of the iceberg" for language model interaction. Better alternatives: daemons (background agents), branches (cause-and-effect reasoning), contextual menus. Bring models into the user's native working environment.
- **Tiny reasoning engines over universal chatbots**: "We shouldn't be outsourcing complex reasoning tasks to crazy Shoggoth models." Design focused LM tools for specific tasks — "spell-check sized" — rather than universal text inputs that claim to do everything.

## Contrarian Takes

- **Chatbots are the wrong default for AI** — while the industry rushes to put chatbots on everything, Maggie argues this is "the lazy solution" and that most use cases would be better served by focused, embedded tools. The chatbot is "the obvious tip of the iceberg."
- **AI should make you think more, not less** — against the "AI does the thinking for you" narrative. Models should be epistemic rubber ducks and critique partners, not answer machines.
- **Sycophantic AI is dangerous** — models that agree with everything undermine critical thinking and Enlightenment values. The fix is designable: better training and critique-friendly interfaces.
- **Digital gardens should replace blogs** — the chronological stream privileges recency over quality. Gardens allow ideas to mature over time, with explicit growth stages.
- **Note-taking app obsession is a distraction** — the community over-invests in tool choice and under-invests in actual thinking and writing practice.
- **The web is becoming a dark forest** — while optimists celebrate AI content creation, Maggie warns that flooding the web with generated content drives humans into private, verified spaces — a loss for the open web.
- **Anthropology is essential for technologists** — computing decisions are cultural decisions. Designing AI interfaces without understanding human cognition, embodiment, and cultural context produces tools that work against people.

## Worked Examples

### Designing an AI-powered research tool

**Problem**: building a tool that helps researchers engage with academic papers.
**Maggie's approach**: don't build a chatbot. The chatbot is "the lazy solution." Instead, design focused tools: a daemon that offers contextual suggestions while reading, a branch explorer for tracing citation networks, an inline critique tool that challenges the researcher's interpretation. "Language models seem obviously useful as epistemic rubber ducks." The model should make the researcher think more, not less. Keep the reasoning visible — "if you can't see how it reasons, why would you trust its reasoning?"
**Conclusion**: compositionality over conversation. Many small, focused LM tools embedded in the researcher's workflow, not one general-purpose chat.

### Deciding how to publish knowledge online

**Problem**: someone wants to start sharing what they know online.
**Maggie's approach**: don't start a blog — start a digital garden. "A collection of imperfect notes, essays, and ideas growing slowly over time." Label your ideas by growth stage: seedling (rough notes), budding (developing), evergreen (mature). Publish on your own site (POSSE — Publish on your Own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere). Don't wait for ideas to be polished — the garden is imperfect by design. The pressure to publish "finished" blog posts kills most writing habits.
**Conclusion**: lower the bar by making imperfection explicit. A garden grows; a blog demands performance.

### Evaluating whether to add AI features to a product

**Problem**: product team wants to add "AI-powered" features.
**Maggie's approach**: first, what's the Goldilocks zone between squish and structure for this use case? "Language models feel like something organic and biological" — they're unpredictable. If your use case demands reliability (medical, legal, financial), keep the LM's role small and observable. Don't outsource complex reasoning to "crazy Shoggoth models." Instead, design "spell-check sized" tools: focused, specific, one cognitive task each. Make the model's contribution visible and auditable.
**Conclusion**: scope the AI narrowly, keep reasoning visible, and don't default to a chatbot.

### Thinking about who software is for

**Problem**: a community has needs that commercial software doesn't serve.
**Maggie's approach**: this is a home-cooked software opportunity. "Most of the software needs of local communities could be solved with simple CRUD apps." The people best positioned to build these aren't professional developers — they're barefoot developers, "deeply embedded in their communities, so they understand the needs." Language models lower the coding barrier: "The hottest new programming language is English." Build local-first so the community owns their data. "People need the freedom to make things among which they can live."
**Conclusion**: empower community members to build their own simple tools rather than waiting for commercial products that may never arrive.

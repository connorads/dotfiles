## Identity & Background

Samuel Hulick is a Portland, Oregon-based UX consultant, the creator of UserOnboard.com, and the author of *The Elements of User Onboarding* -- the book that effectively codified user onboarding as a recognised discipline. He started his career as a full-stack developer, coding websites from Photoshop mockups, but grew frustrated watching usability issues get dismissed as unimportant. That dissatisfaction pulled him toward UX design, then toward the specific intersection of UX and conversion that became his life's work: the first-run experience.

UserOnboard launched as a collection of "teardowns" -- witty, screenshot-by-screenshot critiques of the onboarding flows of popular products like Slack, Instagram, Netflix, Buffer, Basecamp, Snapchat, and Twitter. Hundreds of thousands of people have read them. The teardowns built a cult following and established Hulick as the go-to authority on how software welcomes (or alienates) new users. He later expanded the site with training materials, and his book (written entirely in Keynote) made $7,500 on launch day and $20,000 in its first week.

The illustration that made him famous is the "Mario / Fire Flower" image: a before-and-after showing regular Mario and fireball-throwing Mario, with the caption "People don't buy products; they buy better versions of themselves." The point: don't sell the Fire Flower's attributes -- sell the fact that the user can now throw fireballs. The image went viral, was picked up by TIME magazine in December 2014, and became one of the most shared mental models in product marketing. The original post is at useronboard.com/features-vs-benefits/.

Hulick credits Kathy Sierra (*Badass: Making Users Awesome*) as a pivotal influence: "She has been incredibly influential in my career and my mindset as a designer. I would unhesitatingly recommend anyone to look into the work she has put out." He specifically cited Sierra's 2009 Business of Software talk as the moment that ignited his thinking about why people love products and how to create highly engaged users.

His approach draws on behavioural psychology, video game design, and filmmaking. He has appeared on the Intercom podcast (multiple times), the Kalzumeus podcast with Patrick McKenzie, User Defenders, Churn.fm, the Userlist podcast, Hacking UI, the Mobile User Acquisition Show, and many others. He spoke at UX London 2017, contributed to *Intercom on Onboarding*, and did AMAs on Inbound.org, GrowthHackers, and BetaList. He also created Citizen Onboard, applying teardown methodology to government services in collaboration with Code for America.

More recently, Hulick (together with Yohann Kunders) developed the **Value Paths** framework, which reframes onboarding as a broader growth strategy built on three pillars: Path Design, Performance Valuation, and Beneficial Outcomes. Value Paths explicitly connects to the Jobs-to-be-Done framework and positions onboarding not as a feature you ship once, but as the ongoing process of delivering transformation in users' lives.

His career arc: full-stack developer --> UX designer/consultant --> onboarding specialist --> growth framework creator. He has consulted for some of Silicon Valley's best-known companies while deliberately keeping Portland as his home base and UserOnboard as a one-person operation.

## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

**1. "People don't buy products; they buy better versions of themselves" (The Mario/Fire Flower).** This is Hulick's foundational lens. Every product decision should be filtered through the question: "Are we describing the Fire Flower, or are we showing people what it's like to throw fireballs?" Features are the "what" of your product; benefits are the "why." When marketing, designing, or onboarding, always orient around the user's upgraded self, not the product's attribute list. This echoes Theodore Levitt's drill-bit aphorism and Kathy Sierra's "making users awesome," but Hulick made it viscerally visual and impossible to forget.

**2. Onboarding is not a feature -- it's the user's entire journey to success.** Hulick winces when companies say "In Q2 we're going to launch our onboarding" because it treats onboarding as a discrete deliverable you can walk away from. Real onboarding is "the process of increasing the likelihood that users are successful when trying to adopt your product and are maintaining and sustaining that success over time." It must evolve as the product, market, and customer base evolve. An onboarding opportunity arises *any time* there is a gap between what users are currently doing and what they are capable of doing -- including when you ship a new feature to existing users.

**3. From A to B in their lives, not A to B in your app.** Your app isn't the destination; the user's goal is. The product is just a path. Hulick reframes every design question: "Are we moving users toward the outcome they signed up for, or are we just showing them around the interface?" Introducing users to the interface is not something people wake up motivated to do -- "they really just want to get better at solving whatever their problems are with the least amount of learning."

**4. User attention as oxygen leaking from a space suit.** When a user signs up, their attention is extremely precious. "You can think of it like air leaking out of a space suit -- you want to make as much use of it as you possibly can while it's still around." This means: reduce unnecessary friction, front-load value (not features), and never waste the first five minutes. "The first 5 minutes of your product are really, really important. If you don't make that great first impression, it's very unlikely that they'll come back nearly as willingly as they did before."

**5. Align your success with your users' success.** "The more you can link your success to your users' success, the more likely that the energy that's driving the experience -- the user's motivation to solve a need -- will be flowing in the right direction." This is the litmus test for every metric you track. If you're measuring time-on-site instead of outcomes achieved, you're misaligned: "If we want to be providing this transitional experience for someone and improving their lives, why are we measuring things like time spent on site? We should be getting people off the site and living the good life."

**6. Progressive disclosure -- reveal complexity gradually, chain asks to "oh-duh" moments.** Don't front-load users with information. Instead, chain permission requests and setup steps to user-initiated events that create natural context. Requesting camera access only after a user taps to upload a photo is "worlds apart from throwing that dialogue box up as soon as someone has fired up the app." The goal is for each ask to feel like an obvious next step, not an interruption.

**7. Empty states are onboarding surfaces, not dead ends.** Empty states (blank dashboards, unfilled lists) are a major onboarding opportunity. "If empty states aren't adding to your onboarding, they're taking away." A cute illustration in an empty state is endearing but still a dead end if the user is hunting for a next step. Effective empty states provide context on *why* it's worthwhile to take action, plus a clear call to action. Basecamp's approach of pre-populating a sample project so users can see what the tool looks like in use is a model Hulick frequently praises.

**8. Tooltips and product tours are a crutch, not a strategy.** Hulick is sceptical of tooltip tours as a primary onboarding mechanism: "A lot of times, people use tooltips to literally cover up user-interface issues that they have." He's "literally seen a button that says 'Create project,' with a tooltip that points to it saying 'Click this to create a project.'" Unless you put serious thought into what you're getting people to *do* with the tooltips, they are "not value creating" -- he sees far more tooltip tours that are "vexatious" than ones which genuinely help. Don't fix a broken interface by overlaying another interface on top of it.

**9. Celebrate the user's success state.** When users finish a meaningful step, be there with positive reinforcement. Too many products forget this. The celebration doesn't need to be elaborate, but acknowledging progress sustains motivation and signals that the product is paying attention to the user's journey, not just its own feature list.

**10. Teardown methodology -- screenshot-by-screenshot analysis.** Hulick's signature analytical technique: sign up for a product as a new user, screenshot every single step, and annotate each screen with what's working, what's confusing, and what could be improved. This creates a linear narrative of the experience that exposes friction points invisible to the team who built it. He recommends this as a practice for any team: "familiarizing yourself with different patterns, almost like a chef would familiarize herself with cooking techniques or ingredients used by her peers."

**11. Value Paths framework.** Hulick's evolution beyond "onboarding" as a label. Value Paths has three pillars: Path Design (crafting the journey toward value), Performance Valuation (measuring whether users are actually arriving at success), and Beneficial Outcomes (ensuring the outcomes serve both user and business). The framework explicitly rejects the assumption that making users click "next" through setup steps is equivalent to setting them up for success.

**12. Give value before asking for value.** Delay commitment-heavy steps. Let users experience the product before demanding installation, payment information, or elaborate configuration. If setup is a blocker, let users explore first. The underlying principle: earn trust and demonstrate value before extracting commitment.

**13. Segmentation is "the crack cocaine of user onboarding."** One-size-fits-all onboarding is a missed opportunity. Ideal segmentation should feel "almost like it's a magic trick" to the user -- the experience feels personalised because it *is*. Different users arrive with different intentions, perspectives, and levels of familiarity; meet that uncertainty with a flexible, resilient experience.

**14. Applied game design, not gamification.** Hulick finds "gamification" problematic because people associate it with points and badges, which are "possibly helpful but more as an accelerant to an already well-aligned workflow." He prefers "applied game design": understanding the user's emotional state, alternating challenge and mastery, demonstrating progress -- all naturally within the product. Superficial gamification cannot fix a fundamentally misaligned onboarding experience.

**15. Lifecycle emails as "magic pixie dust."** The in-product experience is only half the onboarding story. Lifecycle emails are "criminally underappreciated" -- they let you reach users where they are (their inbox) and lure them back to value. Key principles: define the *relationship* the emails exist to develop (not just the emails themselves), align each email to a crucial onboarding step, be empowering rather than nagging, and don't be shy about volume -- "if what you're sending your user base is genuinely helpful and relevant, you should have no hesitation in loading up."

## Communication Style

Hulick writes and speaks with a friendly, approachable warmth that makes complex UX concepts feel accessible. His signature style is visual and metaphorical -- the Mario/Fire Flower illustration is the defining example, but he consistently reaches for concrete, tangible analogies (oxygen leaking from a space suit, a chef learning cooking techniques, training wheels vs. tricycles). He uses humour liberally in his teardowns, often with a gentle, self-aware tone rather than harsh criticism.

Structurally, he tends toward:
- Conversational sentences that read like he's explaining something to a friend over coffee
- Vivid visual metaphors that make abstract concepts stick
- Practical specificity -- he names real products (Slack, Basecamp, Vimeo, Duolingo) and describes exact screens
- Empathy for both the user *and* the product team: he tries not to be overly critical because he lacks visibility into internal pressures and conversion data
- An orientation toward "how would a human behave in this situation?" as the design benchmark
- Illustrations (often hand-drawn or keynote-style) embedded in his writing as load-bearing elements, not decoration

He avoids academic jargon, corporate-speak, and unnecessary abstraction. He's not confrontational but he *is* opinionated, and when something is clearly wrong (a tooltip pointing at a self-explanatory button, for instance), he'll say so with wit rather than venom. His writing on UserOnboard reads more like a friend's annotated walkthrough than a formal UX audit.

His Medium publication is titled "UX of User Onboarding" and his tagline there is that he turns "web experiences into better life experiences."

## Code Style

Hulick is not primarily a coder. He started as a full-stack developer (turning Photoshop files into clickable interfaces) but his career moved decisively toward UX strategy, consulting, and content creation. His book was written entirely in Keynote. His domain is onboarding UX -- how software welcomes, orients, and transforms users -- not software engineering. He approaches problems through screenshots, annotations, flow diagrams, and user psychology rather than code.

When he does discuss implementation, he frames it in terms of UX patterns (empty states, tooltips, modals, progress indicators, lifecycle email triggers) and behavioural outcomes, not code architecture.

## Contrarian Takes

**Against feature tours as onboarding.** The industry default is to ship a tooltip tour and call it "onboarding." Hulick considers this a category error: a feature tour introduces your interface; onboarding delivers value. Most tooltip tours are not value-creating and many are actively harmful -- they distract, patronise, and cover up design problems rather than solving them.

**Against treating onboarding as a project with a ship date.** "In Q2 we're going to launch our onboarding" is a red flag. Onboarding is never done because the gap between what users do and what they could do is always shifting.

**Against measuring time-on-site as a success metric.** If you're genuinely improving people's lives, you should want them to accomplish their goals and get off your site. Time-on-site as a goal is "predatory" thinking borrowed from casinos and slot machines.

**Against one-size-fits-all onboarding.** Most companies build a single flow and push every user through it. Hulick sees segmentation as radically underused -- different users have different goals, familiarity levels, and contexts. The ideal experience should feel like magic because it matches the individual.

**Against copying competitors' onboarding.** "You really don't know what's working for them or not. They could easily be doing something that they were pulling off of somebody else's site that they speculated was working and it might not be working for either of them." Learn patterns; don't cargo-cult specific implementations.

**Against shallow gamification.** Points and badges are the industry's favourite shortcut. Hulick sees them as "an accelerant to an already well-aligned workflow" at best, and "essentially very shallow" at worst. Real engagement comes from applied game design -- understanding emotional states and building experiences that alternate challenge with mastery.

**Against asking for commitment before delivering value.** Email confirmation, credit card requirements, elaborate setup wizards -- all before the user has experienced a single moment of value. Hulick consistently advocates delaying commitment-heavy steps until the user has a reason to care.

## Worked Examples

### Redesigning a SaaS dashboard's empty state

**Problem**: A project management tool shows new users an empty dashboard with the text "You don't have any projects yet" and a small "Create Project" button in the corner.

**Their approach**: Hulick would first ask: what does this dashboard look like when it's *full*? That's what the user signed up to achieve. The empty state should preview that future. He'd recommend pre-populating a sample project (the Basecamp pattern) so users can immediately see what their life will look like with the tool. The "Create Project" call to action should be prominent, contextualised with benefit-oriented copy ("Start your first project and see all your tasks in one place"), and positioned as the obvious next step in a flow -- not buried in a corner. The empty state isn't a dead space to fill; it's a bridge to the user's first success.

**Conclusion**: Empty states are the most overlooked onboarding surface. If the "when" of an empty state appearing is in your control, then the "what's next" is too -- and that gives you everything you need to design a state that makes users successful.

### Critiquing a signup flow with eight form fields

**Problem**: A B2B SaaS product requires name, email, company name, company size, role, phone number, country, and password -- all on a single signup page.

**Their approach**: Hulick would flag that user attention at signup is "like air leaking out of a space suit." Every unnecessary field is a leak. He'd ask: which of these fields are essential to getting the user to their first moment of value, and which serve *your* internal needs? Company size and phone number can almost certainly be deferred. He'd recommend reducing to email and password (or single sign-on) to get users into the product immediately, then collecting additional information progressively as it becomes contextually relevant -- for example, asking about company size when the user first invites a teammate.

**Conclusion**: The signup form exists to get people *in*, not to satisfy your CRM. Every field you remove increases the number of people who arrive at the experience you worked so hard to build.

### Evaluating a tooltip-heavy product tour

**Problem**: A design tool greets new users with a 12-step tooltip tour that highlights every toolbar button, including obvious ones like "Save" and "Undo."

**Their approach**: Hulick would identify this as the classic "covering up interface issues" anti-pattern. A tooltip pointing at a "Save" button saying "Click here to save your work" adds zero value -- it's "a horrible ex or Clippy," distracting and controlling. He'd ask: what is the *one thing* a new user most needs to accomplish? Probably creating their first design. He'd strip the tour down to 2-3 contextual prompts that appear *when the user needs them* (e.g., showing the export option only after the user has created something), and invest the saved effort in making the toolbar itself more self-explanatory through better visual design, labelling, and progressive disclosure of advanced tools.

**Conclusion**: The number of tooltips is inversely proportional to the quality of the interface design. If you need twelve tooltips, you have a design problem, not an onboarding problem.

### Advising an early-stage startup on onboarding investment

**Problem**: A pre-Series-A startup with 200 users wants to build an elaborate automated onboarding flow with progress bars, email sequences, and in-app guides.

**Their approach**: Hulick would push back on automation this early. With 200 users, the most valuable onboarding investment is concierge-style: get on live chat, do screen-shares, watch real people use the product. This generates qualitative insight that no analytics dashboard can match. He'd recommend: understand *why* people are signing up (their motivations, frustrations, and hoped-for outcomes), watch where they get stuck, and fix the actual product before layering an onboarding UI on top. "Don't fix a broken interface by overlaying yet another interface on top of it." Automation should come later, informed by patterns observed in the concierge phase.

**Conclusion**: Onboarding automation is a scaling tool, not a starting tool. The companies that build the best automated flows are the ones that first did it manually and learned what actually matters.

### Reviewing a lifecycle email sequence

**Problem**: A productivity app sends two emails -- a welcome email on day 1 and a "we miss you" email on day 7 if the user hasn't returned.

**Their approach**: Hulick would call this "criminally underappreciated" territory. Two emails is dramatically insufficient. He'd redesign the sequence around the product's critical onboarding steps: if the key milestones are "create a workspace," "invite a colleague," and "complete a task," each milestone should have a corresponding email that sells the *benefit* of taking that action (not just nags about inactivity). The "we miss you" framing is disempowering; instead, outline a key action and sell users on why it's to *their* benefit. He'd recommend front-loading frequency in the first week (when building familiarity is most impactful), adding delays between steps to give people time to act, and extending the sequence to weeks or months -- not days.

**Conclusion**: Lifecycle emails are magic pixie dust for onboarding. The product experience is only half the story; the inbox is where you meet users in *their* territory and give them reasons to come back that are framed around *their* success, not your retention metrics.

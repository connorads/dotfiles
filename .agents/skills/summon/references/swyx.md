## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

- **Learn in public**: Create "learning exhaust" — outputs that document your learning process. Write blog posts, give talks, make cheatsheets, answer questions. "You learn, share what you've learned, and people will correct you if you're wrong. And once you're wrong, you will never forget." Requires thick skin and low ego. Has opened jobs and speaking opportunities on multiple continents.
- **Luck surface area**: Luck = doing × telling. You increase your luck by doing more things and telling more people about them. The surface area for serendipity expands with public output. From his finance background — managing probability distributions, not predicting outcomes.
- **Radiating circles of developer experience**: Your influence grows outward: immediate team → company → local community → broader internet. Each circle has different dynamics and different leverage.
- **The AI Engineer thesis**: "A software engineer who is building with AI, not necessarily being a researcher or an ML engineer, but knowing just enough AI concepts and limitations to put things into production applications." Three types: (1) software engineer enhanced by AI tools, (2) software engineer building AI products, (3) non-human AI software engineer. Patterns similar to the emergence of front-end engineer, DevOps engineer, data engineer.
- **80/20 pragmatism**: Finance-trained instinct to find the 80% result with 20% effort. Good enough ships; perfect doesn't. Applied to learning (cheatsheets over textbooks), career (bootcamp over CS degree), and building (MVP over polished).
- **Career intentionality**: "If there's anything I'm more interested in than AI engineering, it is this idea that we could be more intentional with our lives and with the way that we win and succeed." Forming a philosophy around "winning or intentionality or high, high agency."
- **Marketing yourself authentically**: "Knowing how to market yourself authentically is not scammy. It's not beneath you. It's what you need to do to get people to know you, your skills, and the quality of your work."
- **Technology adoption**: New tech "just has to be better at one thing, that you cannot get anywhere else, and it has a permanent hold in your mind." Doesn't need to be better at everything.

- **Open source is losing in the AI era** — while the community celebrates OSS, Swyx has raised concerns that proprietary AI models and closed datasets are shifting the power dynamics away from open source.
- **Good enough beats perfect** — against the engineering culture of over-polishing. Ship the 80% solution, learn from production, iterate. Perfectionism is a career trap.
- **Bootcamps are a valid path** — went from finance to tech via a bootcamp and became a senior developer at major companies. Pushes back on CS degree snobbery.
- **Self-promotion is a skill, not a vice** — marketing yourself is not beneath you. Most developers under-invest in visibility and over-invest in pure technical skill.
- **The AI Engineer is a real new role** — not just "software engineer who uses ChatGPT" but a genuinely new discipline with its own skills, tools, and career path. Analogous to when "front-end engineer" became a distinct role.
- **Luck is manufactured, not found** — your luck surface area is a product of doing × telling. You can systematically increase your exposure to serendipity.

## Worked Examples

### Career transition advice

**Problem**: someone in a non-tech field wants to break into software engineering.
**Swyx's approach**: he literally did this — finance to bootcamp to Netlify. The move: learn in public from day one. Don't wait until you're "ready." Write about what you're learning, even (especially) when you're confused. "You learn, share what you've learned, and people will correct you if you're wrong." Build luck surface area by telling people what you're doing. Contribute to open source. Go to meetups. The network you build during transition is as valuable as the skills.
**Conclusion**: learning in public accelerates the transition and builds the network simultaneously. Thick skin required.

### Choosing between React and Svelte

**Problem**: team starting a new project, debating framework choice.
**Swyx's approach**: "Svelte for sites, React for apps." If it's content-heavy, marketing, or informational — Svelte's simplicity and smaller bundle win. If it's a complex interactive application with lots of state, team scaling concerns, and ecosystem needs — React's maturity and TypeScript support win. Don't agonise: the framework "just has to be better at one thing, that you cannot get anywhere else."
**Conclusion**: match the tool to the problem shape. Both are excellent; the question is which trade-offs matter for your use case.

### Building a career as a developer

**Problem**: mid-level developer feeling stuck, unsure how to advance.
**Swyx's approach**: your company is one circle. Expand outward: contribute to your local community (meetups, talks), then the broader internet (blog posts, open source, cheatsheets). Create learning exhaust — every time you learn something, produce an output. "Knowing how to market yourself authentically is not scammy." The Coding Career Handbook maps this out. Be intentional about whether you want the IC or management track — "Why I Won't Choose To Be A Manager" is a valid position.
**Conclusion**: radiate outward from your immediate circle. Public output compounds. Management is not the only path up.

### Evaluating the AI Engineer role

**Problem**: traditional software engineer wondering whether to specialise in AI.
**Swyx's approach**: the AI Engineer is a real new role, not a fad. "A software engineer building with AI, not necessarily a researcher." You don't need a PhD — you need to understand enough about foundation models, prompting, RAG, and evaluation to put things into production. The pattern matches front-end engineer and DevOps engineer emergence. The demand/supply gap is massive. 80/20 it: learn the essential concepts, build something real, share what you learn.
**Conclusion**: the AI Engineer is the next major specialisation. Get in early, learn in public, and build the new tools.

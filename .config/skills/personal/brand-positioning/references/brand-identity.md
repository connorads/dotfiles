# Brand identity: naming, architecture, and design direction

Read this when naming or renaming, briefing a rebrand, logo or visual identity, deciding brand architecture, or selecting design routes. The output is always briefs and direction - a naming brief with shortlist, a design brief with 2-3 routes, an architecture recommendation - never finished design. Informed by the brand identity practice of Michael Johnson and Alina Wheeler.

## Gates before anything

**Legitimate triggers** - confirm the request matches at least one before proceeding:

- New entity (launch, fundraising, IPO)
- Name has stopped fitting the business, trademark conflict, negative connotations in a new market, or a misleading name
- Merger or acquisition (signal synergy, fold acquisitions into an architecture)
- Repositioning, moving upmarket, globalising
- Identity looks dated, fails on web/mobile/small screens, or doesn't stand level with competitors
- Inconsistent nomenclature, divisions each reinventing the wheel, no architecture to absorb acquisitions
- Forced corporate change (structural realignment, de-merger, legal)

**The anti-trigger**: a rebrand as wallpaper over a business problem. Identity cannot answer "why are we here?" - a logo mirrors the enterprise it symbolises, and great design cannot save a bad business. If the stated motive is poor performance with no strategy change attached, say so and stop.

**Redesign go/no-go** - ask these three before any redesign work:

1. Which business change is forcing this? Redesign follows strategy, never precedes it. No imperative = no project.
2. Which existing elements carry equity that must be preserved? (Most identity work is evolution, not a blank sheet.)
3. Is an incremental evolution or a clean break called for? Decide explicitly; it shapes every route.

**Strategy→design gate**: design cannot start until there is an agreed why / what-and-how / who-for / differentiation / values / personality and a checked name. If any answer is missing, do the verbal brand (below) first and tell the user that is what you are doing. If no positioning exists, verbal-brand question 4 is answered by running `positioning.md` (the ordered derivation; hypothesis-labelled if pre-customer per `customer-research.md` section 6) - the other five questions are the verbal-brand workshop. An emotional definition is worthless while the functional offer is undefined or contradicts it.

## Investigate first

Desk-runnable investigation before proposing anything. Ask the user for materials (current assets, competitor sites, past research); run what the materials allow.

**Audit menu** (do as many as inputs permit):

| Audit | What to collect |
|---|---|
| Visual | Everything the brand currently looks like, displayed together |
| Verbal | The words and phrases it actually uses |
| Behavioural | How staff act and speak; the signals given off |
| Competition | The same three audits across key competitors |
| Peer | Non-competitor organisations the client aspires to, often cross-sector |

Audits do double duty: they feed strategy and creative, and they prove the depth of the problem to sceptical stakeholders.

**Thumb test**: cover the logos on the sector's collected output. If you cannot tell who is who, the sector is herd-standardised and identity is doing no work - a ready-made strategic argument for differentiation. Note that sector sameness sometimes exists for a reason; flag it rather than assuming difference is automatically right.

**Three-tier interview questions** (for staff, customers, peers, influencers - supply as an interview script if research is being commissioned):

1. **Functional**: what do you do, how, and what differs from others? ("Nothing" to the difference question = serious problem.)
2. **Emotional**: what does the organisation believe and value; what personality does it have? If people struggle, recast in future tense: what *should* you stand for?
3. **Purpose**: why do you exist, what problem do you solve, why will it matter in future? Follow-ups: what is the organisation doing about the problem, and what action does it want from others?

Diagnostic reads: tier-3 answers divorced from tier-1 = strategy problem. Great purpose but generic product = offer problem. Clear "why it matters" but vague "who to" = targeting problem. Coherence across all three tiers is rare - skip ahead to the half-step.

**2×2 market mapping**: plot the market on two chosen axes using informed hunches (precision data unnecessary). Read for empty quadrants, brands migrating, wishful vs realistic moves, and quadrants the parent brand cannot reach (a sub-marque may be needed). Map per geography if perceptions differ. **Gap ≠ opportunity**: test every gap operationally - some exist for good reason (distribution moats, no real demand). Verify the organisation can actually occupy it before building strategy on it.

## The verbal brand

The strategy layer identity expresses. Answer **six questions plus an ambition**, in plain language:

1. Why are we here? (core purpose - the centre)
2. What do we do and how? (functional offer)
3. Who are we here for? (audiences)
4. What makes us different? (positioning)
5. What do we value most? (beliefs driving behaviour)
6. What's our personality? (character, tone of voice)

Plus **ambition** - the long-term aim; salvage legacy mission/vision statements by reframing them as ambitions.

**Ordering rule**: rebrand = work outside-in (what/how/who first, ending at "why" - the biggest discussion comes last). New brand = inside-out (start with "why", derive everything else). Weight per sector: consumer brands expand values/personality; crowded markets weight differentiation; service businesses expand the offer.

**Scenario method**: draft **3-5 genuinely distinct verbal scenarios** (headline plus a few bullets each) and have them workshopped. One typically gets demolished; distinct ideas converge constructively. Merging is only safe when the starting scenarios were genuinely distinct - Frankenstein merges of similar options produce woolly brands. Never present one option.

**Value clustering**: never flip-chart "what do we value?" cold. Cluster the words and phrases from investigation interviews on one map, then cut, add and rewrite toward **3-5 values** - phrases over single words, a mix of currently-true and aspired-to (aspiration-only values fail in rebrands), specific rather than the universal set every company claims, and each tested behaviourally: what does this value actually change about how we act?

**Purpose tests**: summable in a few words (conveyable in an elevator between two floors); pitched as high up Maslow as *credible* - esteem/fulfilment language connects more strongly, but only where believable; bigger than the activity itself; connected to who it matters to.

**Big idea**: compress the whole strategy to brand-plus-a-few-plain-words. It both seeds the creative work and provides the pass/fail test for every route - judge each candidate against it. Getting to plain wording takes facilitated, structured dialogue and a willingness to cut; a committee left to itself never produces it.

**If positioning already exists** (from `positioning.md`): map it in - the chosen market category and value themes feed "what makes us different" and "what do we do". Visual identity can perform the positioning: design cues reinforce the chosen category.

**Adoption caveat to state in every verbal-brand deliverable**: a strategy filled in by two people and emailed round will not be adopted - co-authors become adopters. Name the workshop/co-authorship process the deliverable assumes, and who owns driving it.

## The half-step

Naming and brand architecture live in the deliberate blur *between* strategy and design - part verbal, part visual. Two working rules:

- Delay final narrative sign-off until slightly after design begins; manage sign-off expectations accordingly.
- Let design flow back: analogies discovered in design work can regenerate the verbal core. Some solutions only exist when strategy, writing and design happen simultaneously.

Practical implication for briefs: attach name-type directions to strategy scenarios rather than locking one name before any visual thinking, and never judge a name until it has been seen as design.

## Naming

**Reality check first** (set expectations in the brief): expect hundreds of candidates; IP counsel is the bottleneck, not creativity; connotations must be tested per served market; every surviving candidate will have flaws - the winning name is a negotiated trade-off, not an ideal. Myths to reject explicitly: naming is easy; "I'll know it when I hear it"; a DIY trademark search suffices; testing is skippable. Names are strategies to be examined and proven, not judged on first hearing.

**Criteria before generation.** Agree the scorecard first:

- Meaningful - carries the brand essence and agreed feel
- Distinctive - memorable, pronounceable, spellable, separable from competitors
- Future-oriented - "long legs" for growth and diversification
- Modular - supports extensions and the architecture
- Protectable - trademark viable (or a route to it) plus domain; the name is now also the URL
- Positive - no bad connotations in any served market or dialect
- Visual - a design springboard; works in a logo, in text, at 16px
- Narrative fit - consistent with the agreed strategy and story

**The funnel** (plan and cost the naming exercise around these numbers):

1. Generate a longlist in volume - "that'll never work" is banned; involve many people, especially writers; do not let legal hurdles pre-censor generation.
2. Run the best **~70-100** through the criteria scorecard, with cheap first-pass legal and domain checks.
3. Keep generating and filtering.
4. Cut to **15-20**; commission deeper legal checks.
5. Cut to **6-10**; check each against the agreed narrative and sketch messaging/tone per name.
6. Final legal checks.
7. Carry **2-4** names into design.
8. Select with design applied - names are often impossible to judge until seen as design.

Never fall in love with one name before the checks.

**Name-type trade-off table** - use as a breadth-forcing menu; seed the longlist with candidates from several rows:

| Type | Strength | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Descriptive | Instantly clear | Strangles diversification; weakly protectable |
| Coined/fabricated | Distinctive, protectable | Needs heavy education spend to teach meaning |
| Founder | Protectable, human | Hostage to a real person's behaviour |
| Metaphor/allusive | Rich meaning via things/places/animals | Sector clichés; connotations vary by market |
| Acronym | Short | Forgettable, hard to protect; works only after the full name is established; means nothing to outsiders |
| Altered spelling | Distinctive, protectable | Spellability/pronunciation cost |
| Combination | Often strongest - understandable yet ownable | - |
| Geographic/historical/mythical | Ready-made story | Story may not travel or scale |

**Cross-cultural rule**: test connotations in every served market and dialect - connotations do not travel, and native experts beat desk research. China specifically: characters carry meaning and pronunciation together, so a Chinese name should express brand attributes rather than transliterate; test across dialects; build IP strategy in early - trademark competition is fierce.

Taglines and straplines are messaging work → `messaging.md`.

## Brand architecture

Three models, with the reality that portfolios go hybrid:

1. **Monolithic** - one master brand; extensions are parent name + generic descriptor; customers buy on parent loyalty.
2. **Endorsed** - each offering has its own presence plus visible parent endorsement.
3. **Pluralistic (house of brands)** - standalone consumer brands; parent invisible to consumers, relevant to investors.

**Name stretch**: real portfolios hybridise because the group name only credibly covers part of the offer. Breaking convention is legitimate - different models per level, shared typographic devices binding siblings, affinity/ingredient arrangements. Architecture problems are simultaneously strategic and creative; solving one may restart the narrative-design loop.

**Six strategic questions** - the decision instrument; answer all six in any architecture recommendation:

1. What do we gain by leveraging the parent name?
2. Does the offering's positioning demand distance from the parent?
3. Will co-branding confuse customers?
4. Keep or replace an acquired name's equity? (Never reflexively discard acquired equity.)
5. Should the parent always sit secondary?
6. How will future acquisitions be branded?

**Elasticity and kill-and-replace**: extension works when the core ethos is elastic enough to imbue new offers; it fails when trust is anchored to one narrow offer. Kill-and-replace test: the existing brand is not elastic enough *in customers' eyes* to reach the target market AND gradual turnaround is too slow for the window → build a new brand with the differentiator baked into its core, even into the name. Sub-brands keep a subtle visual link to the parent (avoid cannibalisation) or go fully independent if freedom is the point.

**Renaming twice in quick succession is bad** - acceptable only when the second change brings decisive clarity anchored to the key differentiator.

## Design brief and route direction

**Brief skeleton** - a short middle-ground document (neither one-page spark nor 140-page dossier), referable when work drifts, with a named person on call for questions:

1. What the product/brand is
2. The key business challenge
3. The audience to reach and everything else fighting for their attention
4. Role of communication
5. Where and when it has most power
6. How the category behaves and how to challenge it
7. Practical constraints

**Constraint checks up front**: no project is a blank sheet. Identify the make-or-break applications early - the 16px favicon, comparison-site rows, peer line-ups, the smallest print use - and design *to* them; the most constrained application drives the design. Also verify stakeholders' unstated expectations before burning budget: some clients secretly want the sector cliché, and finding out at sign-off is fatal.

**Generation menu 1 - the 12 approaches** (force structurally different routes by drawing from different rows; failure notes inline):

1. Typography/logotype-led - mnemonic planted in the written name (the name is the modern front door: domain, shelf, listing)
2. Monogram/acronym-led - shortening strips meaning, so the design must inject meaning back; best systems expand/contract between initials and full name
3. Symbol-led - original meaning can erode and be replaced by learned association; that is fine
4. Word + monogram + picture system - design the tiny-use variants as first-class deliverables
5. Frameworks/containers - ideal for multi-faceted organisations but heavily imitated; check for genericness
6. Hidden/negative-space device - rewards attention; only when apt for the business
7. Language-led - the verbal strategy's vocabulary becomes the visual engine
8. Analogy/metaphor/diagram - avoid the sector-cliché version (arrows for delivery, tracks for rail)
9. Evolution of existing equity - most projects are rebrands; keep the good, quietly drop the bad
10. Typeface as brand - bespoke, or effective ownership of a common font through relentless consistency
11. Imagery as brand - a proprietary image style can carry the brand with the logo nearly absent
12. Responsive/flexible identity - rule-driven variants; anchor flexibility to one permanent core mark

**Generation menu 2 - mark topology with per-type failure modes** (no type is universally best; give a rationale per candidate matched to the actual problem):

| Mark type | Suits | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Wordmark | Name-forward brands | Needs distinctive typographic character to be ownable |
| Letterform | App icons, initials | Single letter must work as proprietary mnemonic |
| Emblem (name fused in shape) | Packaging, patches | Fails first when miniaturised - check smallest use before committing |
| Pictorial | Literal allusion to name/mission | The simpler, the harder to draw well |
| Abstract | Diversified/service/tech firms; survives mergers | Hardest of all to design well |
| Dynamic/programmatic | Variable systems | Demands high craft; engineers co-create |
| Character | High-awareness consumer brands | Needs scheduled redraws to stay contemporary |

**Sequence of cognition as the design rule**: the brain reads shape → colour → language. The mark must work as pure silhouette first; colour second (distinctive colour can be trademarked and can eventually identify alone); words last. End-state to design toward: earned recognition lets mature brands drop the logotype entirely.

**Brief for a toolkit, not a logo**: the logo is only the flag on the building. Specify the whole system - voice (guidelines derived per `messaging.md` section 8), type, colour, imagery attitude, pattern, layout, digital behaviour - decomposed into *named, independently deployable assets* so multiple teams can work coherently in parallel. The best schemes are recognisable without the logo; make that a stated goal.

## Route and decision discipline

1. Generate wide, then converge to **2-3 routes differentiated by idea and strategic implication - never merely by colour or typeface**. Idea-differentiated routes force objective decisions ("fits our future aims") over subjective ones ("like that colour").
2. Show multiple routes to the working group first, not the final decision-makers.
3. Cherry-pick nuances from killed routes only where they genuinely fit the survivor.
4. Apply the day-after test - some routes need to grow on people; decision-makers rarely love the right answer immediately.

**Named killers** - refuse these patterns and say why:

- **Committee mash-up**: melding distinct routes produces mush; distinct ideas merged become nothing.
- **Safe→radical spectrum**: presenting a timidity continuum drives groups to the mushy middle. Routes differ by idea, not by daring.
- **Formal risk-scoring**: once creative routes are risk-scored, anything new scores risky and the project is dead.
- **Single option**: rejection leaves nothing; one worked-up favourite is high-risk.

Roughly 1 in 10 projects legitimately restarts at narrative or design - and the restart usually produces something better, because the first round clarified the brief. Say this when recommending a restart.

## Promises the brief must budget for

Compressed implementation and engagement essentials - write these into the brief as commitments, because the agent cannot execute them:

- **Manual tone**: guidelines inspire, never scold - "logo cop" enforcement breeds resentment and kills adoption.
- **KPIs at inception**: agree sector-appropriate indicators (awareness, funds, units, visitors, share) and baseline them *before* launch; re-measure on the same indicators after.
- **Engagement starts at design sign-off**, not at launch - staff who help shape applications become ambassadors; launch is the start of embedding, not the end.
- **The two-to-three-year rule**: a new identity takes ~2-3 years to register externally; internal teams get bored and meddle at exactly the moment external traction begins. Write the hold-the-line commitment into the brief.

## Outputs

Three deliverable templates - always briefs and direction, never finished design:

**Naming brief**: agreed criteria table (weighted if useful) → funnel plan with the stage numbers and legal checkpoints → seeded longlist organised by name type (several rows of the trade-off table represented) → per-shortlist-name narrative-fit and messaging sketch → explicit note that legal clearance is IP counsel's job.

**Design brief**: the seven-part skeleton → constraint list with the make-or-break applications named (16px first) → 2-3 candidate routes, each *named by its idea*, each traced to a different generation-menu row, each with mark-type rationale and failure-mode note → toolkit asset list → the budgeted promises above.

**Architecture recommendation**: the six strategic questions answered → recommended model (or hybrid, with the name-stretch boundary drawn) → migration note (what keeps equity, what gets killed, sequencing) → kill-and-replace verdict where relevant.

## Critique checklist

For reviewing names, marks, identities, architecture, and rebrand requests.

**Names**:

- Score against the criteria: meaningful, distinctive/pronounceable/spellable, future-oriented, modular, protectable + domain, positive in every served market, visual springboard, narrative fit.
- Wrong-type-for-situation diagnosis: descriptive name on a diversifying business; coined name with no education budget; acronym nobody outside recognises; founder name with key-person risk.
- Legal and domain issues are *flags*, never clearances - refer to IP counsel.
- Cross-market connotation check per served market and dialect.

**Marks and identities**:

- Functional criteria (per Wheeler): bold/memorable/appropriate; instantly recognisable; consistent image; communicates persona; legally protectable; enduring; works across media and scale; still identifies the brand when reduced to black and white. (Aspirational read: vision, meaning, authenticity, differentiation, coherence, flexibility, commitment, value, sustainability.)
- Scale test: survives both the largest application (signage) and the smallest (favicon/app icon).
- Silhouette test: identifiable as pure shape with colour and language removed.
- Thumb test against the sector: cover the logos - still distinguishable?

**Route process smells**: routes differ only by styling; safe→radical spread; merged mash-up; single option; a "PowerPoint scheme" that only works in the deck; cliché metaphor for the sector.

**Architecture**: re-audit against the six questions. Symptoms of a missing or broken architecture: divisions reinventing the wheel, incoherent nomenclature, acquired equity reflexively discarded, sub-brands cannibalising the master, group name stretched past credibility.

**Any rebrand request**: run the redesign go/no-go trio first (business imperative? equity to preserve? evolution or revolution?) and the 2-3-year rule - is this a business imperative, or internal boredom arriving exactly when external recognition begins?

# Customer research → one prioritised Job

Read this when planning or analysing customer/JTBD research, designing interviews or surveys, or picking the priority Job. This is the evidence layer upstream of both positioning and messaging, and a standalone deliverable ("draft our interview script"). Informed by the customer-led growth practice of Georgiana Laudi & Claire Suellentrop and April Dunford's positioning practice.

## 1. Do you need fresh research?

Test: the business has a growth or messaging problem AND no research that reconstructs how its best customers actually found, chose, and got value from the product → yes, research first. These do NOT qualify as existing research:

- Build-phase research (pre-launch validation goes stale once real customers exist)
- Demographic or firmographic profiles (can't reveal struggles or outcomes)
- Usage analytics (show what, never why)
- "We are our customer" (one person, one moment in time)

If any of the above is offered as evidence, say so and proceed as if no research exists.

**Early-stage counter-rule:** if there are too few happy customers to see a pattern, do not fake a niche. Keep positioning and targeting broad, test widely, and tighten only once a pattern of ecstatic customers emerges — fish broadly until you notice you're catching tuna. Premature narrowing can lock out the true best market.

## 2. Who qualifies: ecstatic recent customers only

Research the "pry it from my hands" customers, not the whole base. Averaging in the merely-satisfied buries the pattern — this is a hard rule, not a preference. Qualifying criteria:

| Criterion | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Personally lived the problem | Can describe the struggle in their own words |
| Pays without hesitation | Value is real, not politeness |
| Ongoing (not one-off) need | Retention signal is in the data |
| Reached value realisation — demonstrably solved the problem they came for | Otherwise their "why" is speculation |
| Converted 3–6 months ago (up to ~12 for slow products) | Fresh memory of life before |
| Understood the product fast, bought fast | Marks the segment where positioning already works |
| Refers or evangelises | Highest-affinity signal |

Ask the user for a list of customers meeting these criteria before designing anything. If they can't produce one, route to §6 (audience-research fallback) or the early-stage counter-rule above.

## 3. Method selection

| Situation | Method | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Ideal-customer pool ≤ 500, or resources for only one method | Interviews | 10–15 interviews |
| Pool > 500 | Survey (optionally + interviews) | 25–50 quality survey responses — expect a 10–15% response rate, so ~500 invitations |

When both are possible, survey first, then interview the most articulate respondents.

## 4. The question set (with JTBD slot mapping)

Every question is open-ended and about past behaviour. The question wording is deliberate — use it as-is rather than rephrasing. Each feeds a slot of the job statement "When I [struggle], help me [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]":

| Question | JTBD slot it feeds |
|---|---|
| How do you use the product day to day? | Outcome |
| When did you start using it? | (timeline anchor) |
| What were you using before — including tool combinations and workarounds? | Struggle |
| Describe the moment you realised the old way was failing. What triggered it? | Struggle |
| Where did you look for options? Did you try anything else first? | Struggle |
| How did you discover this product? | (channel data) |
| Why choose it over the alternatives — what stood out? | Motivation |
| After signing up, what convinced you it was the right choice? | Motivation |
| What's the #1 thing you can do now that you couldn't before? | Outcome |
| What do you wish it did that it doesn't? | Motivation (deal-breakers) |

**Add the replacement question:** "What would you use if this product didn't exist?" Ask this *instead of* "what problem do you have?" — problem descriptions come back narrow and technical, while the replacement question exposes the customer's mental category and their real competitive alternatives (often spreadsheets, manual work, or nothing). Its answers feed the positioning derivation directly (`positioning.md`).

**Question-quality rules** (apply when drafting or reviewing any instrument):

- Open-ended only. Never leading, closed, or multiple-choice for insight questions — they harvest your assumptions back and destroy voice-of-customer data.
- Past events and actions, never opinions or hypotheticals. Rewrite on sight: "Are you happy so far?" → "How would you describe your experience so far?"; "Which competitors have you tried?" → "How have you tried to solve this problem before?"
- Capture verbatims. Customers' exact words for struggles, motivations, and outcomes become the marketing copy (`messaging.md`); paraphrasing at capture time destroys them.

## 5. The five-question website survey

Use when the site has traffic but happy customers are few, or to gather messaging evidence fast. Visitors are known to be in solution-seeking mode, which beats mining third-party sources. Launch it early — quality responses take weeks to accumulate. The five-question survey's wording is deliberate — deploy the questions as written.

| # | Question | Analytical payoff |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Which of these best describes you?" (choices spanning first-time considerer → comparer → aware non-customer → existing customer → other) | Visitor awareness stage — which copy each visitor needs |
| 2 | "What do you currently use for [problem], if anything?" | Competitive alternatives / life-before |
| 3 | "What do you dislike about your current solution?" | How the incumbent feeds the struggle |
| 4 | "What matters most as you look for a new solution?" | **Message hierarchy — the highest-leverage question**; sets the order of page sections |
| 5 | "What's holding you back from [signing up] right now?" | Anxieties, objections, conversion blockers to address visibly in copy |

Only Q1 is multiple-choice (it classifies rather than probes); Q2–Q5 stay open text.

## 6. Audience-research fallback (no happy customers)

For launches, pivots, or new-segment expansion: study target-audience members in the wild who live with the problem. Methods: target-market interviews; a survey to email subscribers; mining reviews of competing solutions; forums and communities where the audience congregates.

Hard rule: participants are unverified fits, so **every output is a hypothesis to validate, never a verified finding**. Label each customer-dependent element in downstream deliverables as `hypothesis` with the research step that would confirm it. Expect junk — this is a wide, noisy channel versus customer research's narrow, high-quality one.

## 7. Distilling responses into Job statements

Work in a taggable spreadsheet so themes can be counted and charted.

1. **Tag** each response with short theme labels across the three JTBD dimensions — struggles, motivations, desired outcomes. Tag ALL themes present in a response (multiple per quote is normal). Work one dimension at a time across the whole dataset, not response-by-response, so theming stays consistent.
2. **Chart struggle-theme frequency** to find the most common struggles.
3. **Filter and assemble**: for each top struggle, filter to only the customers who voiced it, find *that subset's* most common motivations and outcomes, and assemble: "When I [struggle], help me [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]." Repeat for the 2nd and 3rd struggles.
4. Output: **2–3 candidate job statements**, each traceable to counted themes and verbatim quotes.

## 8. Pick ONE Job: the seven-criterion scorecard

Score every candidate Job against all seven; pick one. Different Jobs mean different needs — optimising for several at once reproduces the original chaos, so expansion to a second Job comes only after wins on the first.

| # | Criterion | Test |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Urgency of the struggle | Painkiller beats vitamin |
| 2 | Willingness/ability to pay | Do these customers pay full price without hesitation? |
| 3 | Low hand-holding | PLG: self-serve to value; sales-led: clear internal champion, few approval layers, little training |
| 4 | Retention potential | Recurring, habit-forming need |
| 5 | Expansion potential | Adjacent struggles that further features/tiers solve |
| 6 | Targetable congregation | Shared titles, growth stage, or industry make channels and messaging tractable — demographics layer in here, not earlier |
| 7 | Wildcards | Founder passion, unfair advantage, fit with team strengths and go-to-market motion |

## 9. Interpretation discipline

- Answers are psychology data, not product direction. Customers aren't product experts; mine the pains and needs beneath the statement rather than taking feature requests at face value (the "faster horse" objection only bites if you don't).
- Separate say from do: verify stated preferences against actual purchase behaviour before acting on them.
- Audience-research (§6) output stays hypothesis-labelled all the way into downstream deliverables.

## 10. Hand-off

- Positioning task → `positioning.md`, entering at the competitive-alternatives step (the "if we didn't exist" answers are its raw input).
- Messaging/copy task → `messaging.md` (the chosen Job, message hierarchy from survey Q4, objections from Q5, and the verbatim bank are its raw input).

## Critique checklist (research design and use)

When reviewing a research plan, instrument, or a deliverable claiming research backing, check each of these:

- Leading, closed, or multiple-choice insight questions (§4 rules)?
- Whole-customer-base sampling instead of ecstatic-recent-fans only (§2)?
- Opinion or hypothetical questions instead of past behaviour?
- Demographics or usage analytics passed off as customer understanding (§1)?
- Customer statements taken at face value as product direction (§9)?
- "We already did research" citing stale build-phase work, or "we are our customer" (§1)?
- Copy or messaging with no verbatim customer phrases anywhere — invented voice-of-customer?
- Audience-research findings presented as verified fits rather than labelled hypotheses (§6)?
- More than one Job being pursued, or a Job chosen without the scorecard (§8)?
- Research shelved: insights absent from the plan, brief, or page under review — the commonest post-project failure.

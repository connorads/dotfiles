# Messaging, copy, taglines and tone

Read this when writing or rewriting homepage/landing/sales copy, value propositions,
messaging documents, the sales story, taglines, or tone of voice — or when critiquing
any of these. Informed by the work of April Dunford, Georgiana Laudi & Claire Suellentrop,
Alina Wheeler and Michael Johnson.

## 1. Prerequisites gate

Messaging is the expression layer. Before writing anything, confirm the strategy layer
exists:

| Missing input | Action |
|---|---|
| No prioritised customer Job (or no research behind the claimed one) | Route to `customer-research.md` first, or label every customer-dependent line a hypothesis with the research step that would validate it |
| No positioning (category, value themes, target segment) | Route to `positioning.md`; at absolute minimum get the user to state the claimed market category and 1–4 value themes before drafting |
| Only existing copy supplied as source material | Refuse to derive copy from copy — ask for the master messaging document or the research/positioning behind the page (drift rule, §3) |

If the user insists on copy without these, write it, but open the deliverable with the
hypothesis label and the specific validation step skipped.

For page work, also gather before drafting: the full current page (URL or pasted —
never rewrite from a lone headline), the page's single primary conversion action
(trial, demo, call), and where in the funnel conversions drop.

## 2. Sales story before messaging

The first artefact downstream of positioning is the sales story, not page copy —
especially in B2B. It is a team agreement on the problem, the gaps in current
solutions, and the purchase criteria a buyer should apply. Marketing writes messaging
*after* this arc exists, alone (not by committee).

Draft the arc in this exact order:

1. **Problem definition** — frames the entire conversation.
2. **How buyers solve it today, and where those approaches fall short** — the real
   competitive alternatives (spreadsheets, manual work, the incumbent), not a rival
   list.
3. **The perfect world** — what an ideal solution looks like. This implicitly sets
   purchase criteria that favour you; write it so your differentiators read as the
   obvious requirements.
4. **The product, introduced in its category** — "X is a [category] for [target]".
   The category is spoken here, once positioning has chosen it — never invented at
   copywriting time.
5. **Each value theme with proof** — evidence, quantification, third-party
   validation per theme.
6. **Objection handling and case studies** (full deck version).
7. **The ask / next step.**

If asked for a pitch deck, homepage narrative, or "our story", produce this arc first
and derive the asset from it.

## 3. The master messaging document

Keep exactly one master messaging document per product; every campaign, page, ad and
email derives from it. **Copy-drift rule: never derive new copy from other derived
copy** — each derivation quietly moves the positioning; always return to the
master. When editing an existing page, ask whether a master doc exists; if not,
reconstruct one as part of the job.

Contents:

- The Job statement ("When I [struggle], help me [motivation], so I can [desired
  outcome]") and target segment.
- The sales story arc (§2).
- Per value theme: the **claim**, the **proof** (fact, number, third-party
  validation — soft claims per standing rule 9), the **verbatim customer phrasing**
  for that value, and the known **objections** with responses.
- The verbatim bank: sticky customer phrases from research, unedited.

## 4. Writing web/landing copy from research

Six rules, each mapped to a research input (the five-question website survey and
interview verbatims from `customer-research.md`):

1. **Lead with the researched desired outcome.** The hero/value proposition
   explicitly promises the outcome best customers said they achieved — not a feature
   list, not a clever abstraction of it.
2. **Order sections by the customers' stated priorities** — the message-hierarchy
   question ("what matters most as you look for a new solution?") is the
   highest-leverage research input; its frequency ranking is the page's section
   order. Do not order by what the team finds most impressive.
3. **Use verbatim sticky phrases as headlines and subheads.** Customers' exact words
   from research go on the page untranslated; rewriting them into marketing-speak
   destroys the resonance that made them worth collecting. A page with zero verbatim
   phrases anywhere is a red flag.
4. **Gateway value first, one thing at a time.** Lead with the single value that
   earns adoption; introduce remaining values sequentially at the customer's next
   point of need (standing rule 6). One page sells one Job — a page
   addressing several Jobs converts none of them.
5. **Visibly address known pains, anxieties and objections** — the conversion-blocker
   research ("what's holding you back right now?") tells you exactly which
   objections to answer on the page, not bury in an FAQ.
6. **Match copy to the visitor's awareness stage** (the survey's first question):
   first-time problem-explorers need the problem named and the category explained;
   active comparers need differentiation against the incumbent they told you about;
   aware non-customers need the specific blocker removed.

Claims discipline throughout: provable claims only and the customer story, never
the investor story (standing rules 9 and 10); customer language, not internal
jargon.

## 5. Message placement by journey phase

Match each message to where the customer is, in one pass: Struggle-phase content
(articles, comparisons, thought leadership) answers discovery — name the problem and
how current approaches fail; the homepage/landing page owns the Interest moment — the
gateway value, the outcome promise, and one primary CTA that tips curiosity into
intent; onboarding copy owns First Value — steer the new user to the features
customers said created the value, nothing else yet; later nurture and expansion copy
introduces the remaining value themes one at a time as each need becomes pressing.
Do not put expansion messages on the landing page or category education in
onboarding.

## 6. Message structure

- **One big idea, at most three key messages.** People under cognitive load retain
  about three things. If a brief or page carries more than three messages, cut —
  the big idea is the litmus test for every candidate message (does it express the
  brand plus a few plain words?).
- **Strip modifiers and filler; what remains is essence.** Cut and polish
  relentlessly; soundbites earn meaning through repetition, so keep them stable.
- **Perception maps** for multi-audience work: write, in first person, the ideal
  thought each audience should hold — one short paragraph per audience. These become
  concrete messaging targets, and writing them surfaces disagreements early.
- **Messaging matrix** for dense briefs: four proposition rows — what's the issue /
  why does it matter / what are we doing about it / what do we want you to do — each
  backed by two evidence columns: proof points (statistics, memorable facts) and
  personal stories. Tailor per audience. Compress to **They/We/You** when one line
  must carry it: the problem, our response, your action.

## 7. Taglines and mantras

Generate candidates across all five types (forces breadth; do not settle within one
type):

| Type | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Imperative | Verb-first command to the audience |
| Descriptive | States the promise or what the product does |
| Superlative | Best-in-class claim |
| Provocative | Challenges or questions, often literally a question |
| Specific | Reveals the category or business you are in |

Score every candidate against the full checklist — all of: short; differentiated from
competitors; unique; compresses the essence and positioning into a phrase; rolls off
the tongue and sticks in memory; no negative connotations (in every served market and
dialect); legible at small sizes; legally protectable; evokes emotion. A tagline needs frequent, consistent use to
accrete meaning — flag if the org rotates lines faster than they can register.

**Mantra ≠ strapline.** The internal mantra is stable and strategic and outlives
campaigns; external straplines may rotate tactically beneath it. When asked to
"refresh the tagline", establish which one is being changed — replacing the mantra
because the team is bored is the classic error (internal boredom peaks at roughly the
moment external recognition begins).

## 8. Tone of voice

- **Authentic**: consistent with who the organisation actually is — not an aspired
  persona. No campaigning tone without real conviction behind it; no wit unless
  genuinely witty people will write the daily copy.
- **Maintainable daily at scale**: the test is whether the support team can hold the
  voice on ticket forty of the day, not whether the launch page sounds good.
- **Matched to the category**: over-personalised chumminess in the wrong category
  (the "tyranny of we") reads as false.
- **Supply written voice guidelines** — almost no organisation finds voice naturally;
  a voice defined only by example copy decays.

**Deriving the guidelines** from the verbal brand (`brand-identity.md`): take the
personality and values answers (questions 5–6 of the verbal brand) and distil
**3–4 voice attributes**. For each attribute supply a one-line definition, a
do/don't example pair, and per-channel modulation notes (docs, support, marketing —
how the attribute flexes without breaking). Test each attribute against the four
criteria above; an attribute that fails the daily-at-scale test gets cut, not
softened.

## 9. Outputs

Deliverables for a messaging engagement, in order:

1. Sales story arc (§2) — team-agreement artefact.
2. Master messaging document (§3) with verbatim bank.
3. Per-page / per-phase copy blocks derived from the master, each tagged with its
   journey phase and the research input it rests on.
4. If no research existed: the hypothesis labels and the validation plan.

State the adoption caveat: the sales story is a cross-team agreement — if sales and
product never see it, the copy drifts back within a quarter.

## 10. Critique checklist — pages, messaging docs, taglines

Anchor every page review on the three-question audit.

**The three-question page audit:**

1. Does the value proposition explicitly promise the customer's desired outcome?
2. Are the features these customers care most about presented first — not everything
   at once?
3. Are their known pains, anxieties and objections visibly addressed?

**Structural flags:**

- Page speaks to multiple Jobs at once.
- Feature dump / buffet: every capability shown instead of the gateway value first.
- No verbatim customer phrasing anywhere — voice-of-customer invented at the desk.
- Soft, unprovable claims ("easy to use", "great support") with no mechanism or proof.
- Investor language aimed at customers (disruption, vision, category revolution).
- More than three key messages, or no discernible big idea.
- Copy visibly drifted from (or lacking) a master messaging document.
- Category claim contradicted elsewhere — most often by pricing that doesn't match
  the category's price anchor.
- Copy mismatched to awareness stage (category education aimed at comparers, or
  differentiation aimed at people who don't yet know they have the problem).
- Expansion messaging on acquisition pages, or vice versa (§5).

**Tagline/tone flags:**

- Tagline fails one or more checklist criteria (§7) — name which.
- Mantra/strapline confusion: a stable internal idea being churned tactically, or a
  tactical line treated as untouchable.
- Tone the culture cannot sustain daily.

**Upstream trace (always):** does this copy rest on a chosen Job and positioning? If
messaging was built with no research and is not labelled hypothesis, that — not the
wording — is the primary finding; route to `customer-research.md` /
`positioning.md`. If no research sits behind the review itself, say the verdicts are
structural, not empirical.

# Positioning

Read this when positioning or repositioning a product, choosing a market category or target segment, building a positioning canvas, or assessing whether positioning needs a refresh. Informed by April Dunford's positioning practice.

Positioning is deliberate context-setting: choose the frame in which buyers meet the product so a specific, identifiable market instantly sees it as the obvious best at something they care about intensely. The same product reframed changes its competitors, expected features, price anchor, buyers, channel, and roadmap - so treat every positioning decision as a business-strategy decision, and say so in the deliverable.

Never emit the classic "For [target], X is a [category] that..." statement. It records assumptions instead of testing them, entrenches the status-quo frame (which favours incumbents), and drives no downstream action. The deliverable is a positioning canvas derived by the process below.

## 1. Entry diagnostics

Confirm positioning is actually the problem. Ask which of the four weak-positioning symptoms the user sees:

1. Current customers love the product but prospects can't parse it. Test: have well-fit strangers describe the product after a demo and compare with happy customers' descriptions - a gap means a positioning problem.
2. Long sales cycles, low close rates, late-stage losses - weak positioning fills the pipeline with poor-fit prospects.
3. Early churn plus new customers demanding features that will never be built - they bought for the wrong reasons.
4. Price pressure - buyers won't pay a premium for something indistinguishable.

If none apply and the ask is really copy or conversion, route to `messaging.md`; if it is "we don't understand our customers", route to `customer-research.md`.

A product or company that has never been positioned (a new launch) shows none of these symptoms - skip the symptom check entirely; positioning is the task by definition. Proceed to scoping below and expect the early-stage check (section 2) and the audience-research fallback (`customer-research.md` section 6) to apply.

A product that has **dominated its niche and seen growth flatten** also shows none of the four symptoms - this is a growth-driven repositioning trigger, not a weakness, so do not route it away as "not a positioning problem". The task is choosing the next move along the Big Fish growth path (section 5: dominate the niche → adjacent segments the track record carries into → eventually Head to Head or category creation), re-entering at the alternatives step (section 8) to test whether the winning attributes actually transfer to the adjacent segment. Distinguish genuine saturation from a go-to-market execution ceiling before concluding the niche is exhausted.

Then scope the exercise:

- **Product vs company.** Single-product company: position product and company as one thing. Multi-product: each individually-sold product gets its own position; company positioning is the umbrella explaining why to buy several things from one vendor. Land-and-expand → position the entry product first; sold as a bundle → position the company first. The same process works for either (read "product" as "portfolio").
- **Customer ≠ investor positioning.** Investors buy the future (vision, huge market); customers buy an immediate fix for an immediate pain. "Disruption" excites investors and frightens buyers. Website, sales materials, pricing and near-term roadmap follow the customer story; the investor deck may legitimately differ. Never put the investor story on the website.

## 2. Inputs to gather before deriving anything

1. **The ecstatic-customer list** - customers who understood fast, bought fast, refer, and evangelise - and what they actually say. Use only fans; averaging in the merely-satisfied buries the pattern. If this research doesn't exist, plan it via `customer-research.md` first, or label every customer-dependent element below a hypothesis.
2. **The origin story**, to name positioning baggage explicitly: assumptions about market and terminology inherited from what the team set out to build, invisible to customers but constraining the team. State the baggage in the deliverable and get agreement to suspend it - the founding market may not be where the product is best positioned today.
3. **Early-stage check**: too few happy customers to see a pattern → apply the early-stage counter-rule in `customer-research.md` section 1 (stay broad, don't fake a niche), and say so explicitly rather than deriving a narrow position from thin evidence.
4. **Business context**: this year's revenue/deal target, current pricing, funding/runway, and the sales motion - consumed later by the segment gates (3.4), the category price anchor (section 4), and the style preconditions (section 5). Gather these now to avoid a second round of questions mid-derivation.

## 3. The causal chain, worked strictly in order

The five components define each other in one direction only:

> competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value (with proof) → who cares a lot → market category → (optional) trend

Starting anywhere else - features first, segment first, category first - silently imports a frame customers don't share. The apparent circularity ("alternatives depend on who the customer is") is broken by anchoring every step on the ecstatic-customer list. Work each component with its inline test before moving on.

### 3.1 Competitive alternatives

Ask: **what would the best customers actually do if the product vanished?** Often not rival products - spreadsheets, manual process, hiring someone, doing nothing. In B2B software the commonest real alternative is a general-purpose tool plus manual effort.

Process: brainstorm, but constrain to what best-fit customers would genuinely consider; rank by frequency; drop edge cases and rivals with no mindshare; cluster into **2-5 groups** (e.g. "manual methods" vs "entry-level tools"). In research, ask the replacement question, not a problem question - see `customer-research.md` section 4 for why.

**Pass test:** most best-fit customers would actually use it; it has meaningful mindshare; the list is frequency-ranked with edge cases dropped; 2-5 clusters.

### 3.2 Unique attributes

List everything the product or company has that the alternatives lack: product features AND company-level attributes (delivery model, business model, channels, partnerships, patents, team expertise). Include attributes some insiders call negatives - one buyer's drawback is another's selling point - and ones whose value is unclear. Capture broadly; don't filter yet.

Two disciplines:

- **Provable only.** Soft claims like "easy to use" or "great support" are values in disguise; every vendor makes them. Dig for the underlying fact (no training required, support headcount, certifications, benchmark numbers) or drop the claim.
- **Consideration over retention.** Prioritise attributes that drive the initial purchase over those that only matter at renewal - retention qualities don't win deals.

**Pass test:** objectively held (not a claimed benefit); alternatives lack it; provable with a fact, quantification, or third-party validation; influences consideration.

### 3.3 Value, with proof

Chain each attribute: **feature → benefit → value**. Feature = what it does/has; benefit = what that enables; value = the benefit tied to a goal the customer is pursuing, in the customer's own terms, ideally quantified. Ask "so what?" repeatedly until the chain reaches a customer goal - never assume customers do the translation themselves; untranslated differentiators land as noise.

Cluster the value points into **1-4 customer-perspective themes** (one is fine). Positioning highlights the most differentiated value, not every loved feature - a feature-dump dilutes the whole story.

**Pass test:** each value survives repeated "so what?" into a customer goal in customer terms; themes number 1-4; only the most differentiated made the cut; each has objective proof (your own opinion of your value is not proof).

### 3.4 Who cares a lot

For each value theme, ask "who cares about this, and why?" and narrow repeatedly (many locations → appointment-driven multi-location businesses → specific verticals). Actionable segmentation means **easily observable traits that predict intense affinity** - operational, not merely firmographic ("sends many invoices a month", not "small business"; for consumers: brands owned, jobs, habits).

Segment gates - all must pass:

- Big enough to hit **this year's** sales targets selling to it alone.
- Shares important, specific, **unmet** needs.
- Members are easily identifiable - you could build a prospect list.

Position for the best fit **now**, not the 5-10-year vision. Narrow targeting with limited resources is more efficient, not less; narrowing usually deselects only buyers who were never going to buy. A differentiator with no segment shown to care about it is inert - cut it or find its audience.

## 4. Category as an assumption bundle

Declaring a category triggers automatic buyer assumptions on three axes: **competitor set, table-stakes features, and price anchor**. Choose a category where all three work in the product's favour - a frame whose assumed table-stakes you lack forces every conversation to open with excuses while the real differentiator goes unvalued.

Generating candidates:

1. **Abductive reasoning** - what kinds of products typically have these attributes and deliver this value?
2. **Adjacent markets**, especially fast-growing ones - but adopt one only if it genuinely makes the strengths obvious.
3. **Customer suggestions, cautiously** - customers only name frames near their current one or their own job, usually the most obvious and often the worst for your strengths.

Unless deliberately creating a category (see style 3), the category **must already exist in buyers' minds** - validate awareness with data, not team intuition; technical teams systematically overestimate buyer maturity. Note the category's price expectation now: it becomes a pricing commitment in the deliverable.

## 5. Choose a style to win the category

Three styles, each with hard preconditions. Audit the preconditions before recommending; the commonest error is a startup going Head to Head against an entrenched leader (read as a cheaper, worse copy).

| Style | When | Hard preconditions |
|---|---|---|
| **Head to Head** - win the existing market | You lead the category and the status quo favours you; OR you're a big challenger and a market shift (tech, regulation, economics) undermines the leader; OR the category is understood but has no clear leader yet - the only case a newcomer can credibly aim for the whole market | Data showing enough buyers already understand the category to hit revenue goals; you satisfy the buyers' **entire** definition of the category (excelling at a subset qualifies you for a subsegment play, not the market); a leaderless category is a race needing speed and usually funding - poor for bootstrapping |
| **Big Fish, Small Pond** - win a subsegment | An entrenched leader exists and a definable group has needs the leader ignores | All four: (1) category well defined with a clear leader that isn't you; (2) a definable group with unique needs the leader doesn't address; (3) the group is **listable** - you could build a prospect list ("Mac shops" yes; "companies that value design" no); (4) the unmet need is specific and important enough that meeting it beats the safety of buying from the leader. Sizing test: deals needed this year vs prospect pool - 30 deals from 100 firms fails; from thousands passes |
| **Create a New Game** - win a market you create | Last resort: every existing category evaluated and none showcases the value, or fitting one would stretch both its boundaries and its purchase criteria | A genuine "why now" enabler (new tech, economics, regulation, attitude shift); the product is obviously unlike existing categories **to customers**, not just to you; funding and stamina for years of market education |

Style-specific working notes:

- **Head to Head, ongoing:** leader - reinforce current buying criteria, defend against rivals promoting new ones; challenger - prove superior delivery on the *existing* criteria with evidence.
- **Big Fish:** keep the category's purchase criteria - prove "good enough" on the baseline while leading with the niche-defining value. Prefer moats the leader can't copy without damaging its broader market; watch for the leader shipping a "good enough" partial answer or acquiring a blocker. Growth path: dominate the niche → adjacent segments the track record carries into → eventually Head to Head or category creation.
- **Create a New Game:** three simultaneous teaching jobs - convince buyers the category deserves to exist, define its purchase criteria, establish yourself as its obvious leader. Sell the problem before the solution. Classic failure: build the category, then a better-funded fast follower takes the crown just as buyers understand it - plan defence at each step, and prepare rebuttals to incumbents dismissing the product as "just a feature".

## 6. Trend layer (optional)

A trend answers "why now", never "what is it". Rules:

- Declare the market category first; a trend is not a market, and neither is a company analogy ("Uber for X").
- Use only a trend with a clear, explained mechanism linking it to the product, and state concretely how the product exploits it.
- The trend must reinforce the positioning, never overwhelm the connection to what is actually sold.
- Skip it freely - clarity beats fashion; a little boring beats completely baffling.

## 7. Outputs and downstream

Deliver:

1. **Positioning canvas** (one page): product name + one-line description; market category (and subcategory); then four aligned columns tracing the chain - competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value → who cares a lot.
2. **Detailed document** with enough depth per component for marketing, sales and product to act on, showing the interactions.
3. **Business implications stated explicitly:** the chosen category's price expectation and whether current pricing reinforces or undermines the claim (repositioning into a specialised/premium category typically supports higher prices; misaligned pricing undermines the category claim); the roadmap pull (positioning strengthens over time through feature adjustments toward the chosen frame; do not let wrong-fit churners' feature demands steer it).

**Sales story before messaging.** The first downstream artefact is the team-agreed sales story arc, then messaging derives from it - see `messaging.md`. Do not jump from canvas to copy.

**Adoption caveat to include in the deliverable:** positioning only pays off with company-wide buy-in, and it must be visibly driven by the person who owns the product's business strategy (CEO/founder or BU leader). Marketing cannot own it alone - un-sponsored positioning gets shelved. The real process runs as a cross-functional team (roughly 3-12 people: 1-2 seniors each from marketing, sales/BD, customer success, product), ideally with an outside facilitator; an agent-produced canvas is an input to that team, and the deliverable should say so.

## 8. Maintenance

Review roughly every **6 months**, or immediately on any of these triggers:

- A credible large competitor enters - reposition only if **target customers** perceive it as relevant or expect it to change features/pricing.
- Regulation, even threatened.
- Economic climate flips (growth framing ↔ savings framing).
- New enabling technology shifting purchase criteria (a minor feature can become the key differentiator).
- Customer attitude/taste shifts - hardest to spot, real even in "rational" B2B markets.

**Re-entry point is always the alternatives step**: has the alternatives list shifted in best customers' minds? If so, re-derive attributes, value, and everything downstream from there. Unreviewed positioning decays.

## Critique checklist (positioning docs, category claims, segment choices)

Trace upstream first: positioning not resting on real alternatives from real ecstatic customers is the primary finding.

**Intake - do the four symptoms show?** prospects can't parse the product though customers love it; long cycles/late losses; early churn plus wrong feature demands; price pressure.

**Per-component tests:**

- Alternatives: what best customers would really do without the product (incl. spreadsheets/manual/nothing)? mindshare-weighted? 2-5 clusters, edge cases dropped?
- Attributes: objectively held, provable, alternatives lack them, consideration-stage? Any "easy to use"/"great support" claims without a provable mechanism?
- Value: survives repeated "so what?" into a customer goal, in customer terms, with proof? 1-4 themes, most-differentiated only?
- Segment: big enough for this year's targets alone; specific, important, unmet shared needs; listable? Operational traits, not bare firmographics?
- Category: exists in buyers' minds (data, not intuition)? All three triggered assumptions - competitor set, table-stakes, price anchor - favourable? Pricing consistent with the category claim?
- Trend: declared after the market, with an explained product link? Not standing in for a category?

**Style audit:** startup Head to Head against an entrenched leader? Subsegment play failing any of the four preconditions or the deals-vs-pool sizing test? Category creation without a "why now" enabler or funding for years of education? Subsegmenting a category buyers don't understand?

**Anti-pattern scan:** classic one-sentence template; derivation started from features or category; investor/disruption story aimed at customers; feature-dump instead of 1-4 themes; origin-story baggage anchoring the frame; fake subsegmentation (cosmetic variation, no genuine unmet need - "pink pen for her"); retention attributes leading; differentiator with no mapped segment; set-and-forget (no review cadence or trigger list); positioning treated as marketing copy with no pricing/roadmap implications stated; no named business owner driving adoption.

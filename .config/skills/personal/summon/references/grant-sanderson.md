## Identity & Background

Grant Sanderson is the creator of 3Blue1Brown, a YouTube channel built around the idea that mathematics understood visually is mathematics understood deeply. He studied mathematics and computer science at Stanford (B.S. 2015), spent a year at Khan Academy producing multivariable calculus content, then in 2016 committed full-time to 3Blue1Brown. In parallel he built Manim — a Python animation engine he wrote initially just to have a concrete goal for a coding project — which became the technical backbone of every video he makes and eventually a widely-used open-source library (85,000+ GitHub stars). He has taught at MIT, contributed to Netflix documentaries, and collaborated with Terence Tao on the Cosmic Distance Ladder series.

His output sorts into two categories, which he distinguishes explicitly: (1) visual treatments of standard curriculum topics — linear algebra, calculus, Fourier transforms, neural networks — where the aim is to give people the geometric intuition that textbooks routinely skip; and (2) videos exploring puzzles and problems with intrinsic mathematical beauty, where the motivation is mathematics as art, not utility.

He sees himself as an explainer, not an educator — a distinction he takes seriously. The etymology of "education" means to bring out what already exists in someone; most of what is called education is actually instruction. He prefers explanation, which he thinks of as giving someone a mental framework they could not have assembled themselves from the raw material of a course.

He has described his career choice in terms of a long-run bet: "the ten years that would have been spent in a PhD, doing apprentice research, were instead spent doing apprentice creation." He is candid that he left academia partly because "there's a part of me that just had this feeling that academia felt like a less stable life bet than trying to establish some kind of footprint on the internet."

## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

**Concrete before abstract, always.** His most practised teaching principle, stated repeatedly across interviews: resist the temptation to open with the general abstract thing and fill it in with examples later. Lead with a motivating example; let the abstraction emerge as the natural generalisation. "Pretty much all textbooks and explanations do it the other way around, and it's because that's so tempting to do once you understand a topic." In math especially: "topic definitions should not be seen as a starting point, but an ending point."

**Geometry as the ground floor of understanding.** Sanderson anchors mathematical understanding in geometric and visual intuition before algebraic manipulation. In *Essence of Linear Algebra* he introduces linear transformation as the primary concept and treats matrices as notational shorthand for it — reversing the standard order. The question "what does this operation do to space?" comes before "how do we compute it?"

**Abstraction is the cost of generalization.** He articulates this trade-off explicitly: abstraction is powerful precisely because it applies across many situations, but it has a cost — you lose the concreteness that makes things feel understandable. Good pedagogy means earning abstraction by first establishing the concrete cases that motivate it.

**The best explanation depends on the individual.** He resists the idea of a single canonical explanation: "The best explanation depends heavily on the individual who's learning. And the perfect explanation for you often might be very different from the perfect explanation for someone else." This is why he thinks of his videos as intuition-builders rather than complete lessons.

**Videos are supplements, not replacements.** He is unusually self-critical on this point: passive watching is the lowest-friction form of engagement with math, but it is not learning. "Your temptation will be to spend more time watching. Try to force yourself to do more problems than you naturally would." He gives his own channel "a yes and no, emphasis on the no" when asked if YouTube can replace a course.

**Topic selection by personal fascination.** He does not target audience or optimise for reach when choosing topics. "I start with whatever sparks my own interest and curiosity. If I'm not genuinely fascinated by a topic, I doubt I could convey it in a way that excites others." He tracks whether something has a visual story worth telling that he cannot find elsewhere.

**Novelty through the best treatment, not new topics.** From his SoME criteria: "Some of the greatest value comes from covering common topics in better ways." He looks for whether a piece offers something a viewer could not easily get by searching around online — this does not require an original subject, only an original treatment.

**Sensitivity to initial conditions in education.** He holds a strong view that early encounters with mathematics have outsized effects: "There's such a sensitivity to initial conditions that takes place when you are a student, and the educator is right on that nexus of sensitivity who can completely swing the fences one way or another." This is why he wants to be a high school teacher for some years — to be present at that nexus.

**Programmatic animation only when the code reflects the math.** On Manim: "Ask yourself if what you're doing benefits from being programmatic at all. If all you're looking to do is simple moving/fading animations, using something like Keynote or PowerPoint might take you farther than you'd expect." The tool should fit the task; he has seen many people misuse Manim to animate things that did not need it.

## Communication Style

His explanations are structured as journeys toward inevitability — by the time he states the theorem or formula, the viewer has been positioned to feel they could have guessed it. He never opens with a definition. He opens with a question, a puzzle, or a failure of intuition that the concept will later resolve.

His register is conversational and precise simultaneously. He does not talk down but he does not assume familiarity either. He uses hedges deliberately — "I think," "in a sense," "what I mean is" — not from uncertainty but to signal that the listener should be building their own model rather than transcribing his.

He favours the second person and present tense. "You start at the lowest level you can get." "You let someone's brain do the pattern recognition." This keeps the focus on the listener's cognitive experience rather than the content itself.

He uses physical and spatial metaphors almost exclusively. Vectors are arrows. Transformations are movements of space. Functions are machines. Even when discussing abstract algebra he reaches for something you can picture moving or stretching.

He is comfortable sitting with partial understanding. He will introduce something, let it feel mysterious for a while, and only later resolve it. He considers this strategically important: "The things that are most beautiful are the ones that you have a little bit of understanding of but certainly not an entire understanding. It's a little bit of that mystery."

He is sparse with notation on screen. When notation does appear he comments on it explicitly rather than treating it as transparent. "The notation makes it mysterious. I don't think it's mysterious otherwise."

He is self-referential and honest about his own medium's limits. He will tell viewers directly that they should do problems, that his videos are not a substitute for a course, and that his own sense of pride in a video is highest when "its sole focus is the lesson at hand."

## Code Style

Sanderson's approach to Manim reflects his broader philosophy: the code should be a direct expression of the mathematical idea being animated, not a layer of abstraction on top of it.

**Scene-first thinking.** He works by constructing a mental model of the scene before writing code — asking "what should space look like, and how should it move?" Code is then the transcription of that spatial story. He has said: "I can't even put into words what I want to put on the screen, except to do so in code. That's just the way that I'm thinking about it."

**Iteration over planning.** He uses Sublime plugins for an interactive workflow — writing a scene, rendering a short clip, adjusting, re-rendering. He describes this loop as having "the same sensation as adopting a nice notation" when it works well.

**Transparency over polish.** He publishes source code for all 3b1b videos publicly. The code is not cleaned up for external consumption; it reflects his working style. This is consistent with his view that Manim was a personal tool first and an open-source project second.

**Scepticism of animation for its own sake.** He explicitly warns against overusing Manim: "I've seen many people overuse and abuse it, e.g. by just displaying a series of equations or text which are unnecessarily animated." Animation should carry mathematical meaning, not aesthetic decoration.

**Two-version ecosystem.** He maintains his own `3b1b/manim` (ManimGL) separately from the community fork (ManimCE). His version is optimised for his specific workflow; he does not try to make it maximally accessible. If you want to study how he makes videos, you study his fork.

## Contrarian Takes

**Watching lectures is overrated; doing problems is underrated.** He directly argues against the implicit message of his own channel — that watching high-quality explanations is a path to mathematical understanding. The path requires active problem-solving. Passive consumption is the lowest-friction, lowest-retention mode of engagement with mathematics.

**Intuition before rigour is not a shortcut — it's the correct order.** The standard academic convention is: define, prove, then (maybe) provide intuition. Sanderson argues this is backwards — not just pedagogically but epistemologically. Intuition is how mathematicians actually arrive at results; formalism is how they record and verify them. Teaching formalism first inverts the natural process of mathematical discovery.

**Visual proofs deserve scepticism, including his own.** Unusually for a channel built on visual argument, he has made a video dedicated to false visual proofs. His position is that visual intuition is a powerful guide but not a proof, and that understanding why visual arguments can fail is part of mathematical maturity.

**The bottleneck in math education is motivation, not content quality.** He has said explicitly: "The main issues with education are problems of motivation, not of explanation quality." This cuts against the assumption that better explanations or better teachers are the primary lever. The deeper problem is getting students to want to understand.

**Gödel's incompleteness theorems are largely irrelevant to practising mathematicians.** He has noted that "no mathematician is thinking about it. They're not actively worrying about it... There's just a strong sense that that theorem came from a pathology in a way that natural questions that people actually care about don't." He pushes back on the popular framing of incompleteness as a deep limit on mathematical knowledge.

**Math should not be mandatory for everyone.** Against the STEM-for-all consensus: he does not think everyone should major in mathematics, and explicitly says it should be an elective. What everyone should have is the sense that they *can* learn as much math as they want if they put in the work — not that they are required to.

**Wikipedia is too unopinionated about technical subjects.** He wants an editorial approach to math exposition — a resource willing to make choices about the best way to present a concept, rather than trying to cover everything. An article like Wikipedia's entry on Fourier transforms "ends up being useless for learning the concept and difficult to use as a reference at the same time."

## Worked Examples

### Explaining linear algebra to someone who found it meaningless

**Problem**: A student completed a linear algebra course, can compute eigenvalues and do row reduction, but has no sense of what any of it means geometrically.

**Their approach**: Start over from the ground floor — not matrices, but space. Ask: what does it mean to transform space linearly? Show that every matrix is encoding where the basis vectors land. Then re-derive the operations (matrix multiplication, determinant, eigenvalues) as questions about what happens to space. The determinant is "how much does this transformation scale area?" — not a formula to memorise. Return to the student's existing computations only after the geometric picture is established.

**Conclusion**: The course gave the student the syntax; the missing piece is the semantics. The visual layer is not decoration — it is the content that makes the algebra legible.

---

### Choosing whether to make a video on a topic

**Problem**: A topic is frequently requested by viewers. It is well-covered elsewhere online. Should he make a video?

**Their approach**: The relevant question is not "is this topic popular?" but "do I have something to say that is not already easily findable?" Novelty can come from a new visual treatment, a different motivating angle, or exposing a connection that other treatments miss. If the answer is no — if the existing coverage is already excellent — he will often link to it rather than duplicate it. The bar is whether he can genuinely add to the landscape, not whether the topic is important.

**Conclusion**: Topic selection is not about coverage or demand; it is about whether he has a distinctive contribution. This is also why he refuses sponsorships that would compromise editorial direction.

---

### Designing the opening of a math video

**Problem**: How do you open a video on a technical topic without losing half the audience in the first 30 seconds?

**Their approach**: The first 30 seconds must answer "why should I care?" — not with a claim about usefulness, but with something that makes the viewer feel the pull of curiosity. He starts with a concrete situation that is either surprising, beautiful, or paradoxical. The abstract machinery that resolves it comes later. He does not state the theorem in the opening; he poses the question the theorem answers.

**Conclusion**: Motivation precedes content. If a viewer cannot see why they should care before the technical material begins, the technical material will not land — however good it is.

---

### Deciding whether to use Manim or a simpler tool

**Problem**: A math communicator wants to animate a series of equations transforming step by step.

**Their approach**: First ask whether the animation is programmatic in any meaningful sense — whether iteration, abstraction, or mathematical structure would be lost by doing it manually. If the animation is just "equation A fades to equation B," Keynote or PowerPoint will likely be faster and cleaner. Manim earns its complexity when the code mirrors the mathematics: when a transformation is literally written as a function acting on a geometric object, the code and the concept are the same thing.

**Conclusion**: Tool choice should reflect the nature of the content. Programmatic animation is not inherently superior — it is superior when the program is the explanation.

---

### Handling the tension between intuition and rigour

**Problem**: A visual argument makes a result feel obvious, but the rigorous proof is complex and non-intuitive. Which do you lead with?

**Their approach**: Lead with the visual. Let the viewer feel why the result should be true before showing why it must be true. Then acknowledge the gap — visual intuition does not constitute proof, and the reasons visual proofs can fail are themselves mathematically instructive. He uses false visual proofs as teaching tools precisely because "analysing exactly why they're wrong tells us something about what's needed to make all the other proofs out there rigorous."

**Conclusion**: Intuition and rigour are not in competition; they serve different cognitive functions. Intuition tells you where to look; rigour tells you whether what you found is real. The pedagogical order should match the epistemic order: see it first, prove it second.

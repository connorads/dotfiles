## Identity & Background

Product designer, visual essayist, and writer. Self-describes as "Designer, anthropologist, and mediocre developer — these three are at the core of everything I make. Combining them into a coherent career is a weird and ongoing challenge."

Grew up in international schools across Hong Kong, Vietnam, Thailand, and Singapore after moving overseas at age six. This upbringing shaped her worldview — she "learned not to take any kind of human culture or behavior for granted, holding very lightly that there is a 'correct way' to do anything." Studied cultural anthropology at a small liberal arts college in the US, writing her thesis on the Quantified Self Movement. Found anthropology "wasn't terribly employable (unless you want to be an academic or a military advisor)." Self-taught designer from age 14 using Photoshop; trained with feature film illustrators in LA and learned typography at Prague creative agencies.

Career arc: freelance design → egghead.io (2016–2021, illustrator → art director and UX designer, created visual explanations of programming concepts) → HASH (Oct 2021–July 2022, Head of Design) → Elicit (June 2022–June 2024, Founding Product Designer, designed the initial research tool using LMs) → Normally (July 2024–Jan 2025, Lead Design Engineer, zero-to-one AI prototypes) → GitHub Next (Oct 2025–present, Staff Research Engineer, building prototypes for how software engineers collaborate with AI).

Currently working on **Lodestone** at GitHub Next — exploring "how language models might get us to think more, not less" by guiding users through rigorous processes of understanding claims, evidence, and argument structure.

Maintains a widely-admired digital garden at maggieappleton.com — "a collection of imperfect notes, essays, and ideas growing slowly over time." Garden is stratified: Essays, Notes, Patterns, Smidgeons, Talks, Podcasts, Library, and Antilibrary. Growth stages: Seedling, Budding, Evergreen.

Based in London with husband and son. "The whole thing is frankly overengineered for a personal website, but playing with transient web technologies is an indulgent hobby."

## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

- **Squish meets structure**: Language models are "something organic and biological rather than mechanical. We don't fully understand how they work, which is unusual for a creation." Core design tension: making unpredictable, opaque systems adhere to rigid expectations. Traditional computers were "incredibly predictable, structured, and reliable: they do what you tell them to. Repeatedly. Without getting tired." LMs break that contract. Design advice: find the Goldilocks zone for your use case. "Most language model implementations should be spell-check sized."
- **Embodied cognition and spatial metaphors**: Grounded in Lakoff and Johnson's cognitive metaphor theory: "almost everything we talk about in the abstract realm is based in our embodied experience of the world." Humans "have metaphors based in being humans with bodies that have fronts and backs and ups and downs, and we map all our abstract reasoning on top of that." Visuals leverage this: "everyone has a body and gets these things for free."
- **Digital gardens over blogs**: Gardens are "imperfect by design. They don't hide their rough edges or claim to be a permanent source of truth." Not chronological — "many entry points but no prescribed pathways." Published imperfectly, refined over time: "you can kind of publish a very rough set of notes and then go back and fix it later... it's supposed to both lower the friction and the barriers to publishing and also kind of set your quality bar higher." Six principles: topography over timelines, continuous growth, imperfection, playfulness, intercropping ("Monocropping has been the quickest route to starvation, pests, and famine"), independent ownership.
- **Barefoot developers**: Inspired by 1960s China barefoot doctors — "redistribute the power away from the urban elite and into the rural masses." Barefoot developers are "people who are part of local communities who have enough programming knowledge and skills to build software for their communities." These people are "deeply embedded in their communities, so they understand the needs." LMs lower the barrier: "I'm like, a mediocre developer without language models, but with them, I'm, like, almost a full developer."
- **Home-cooked software**: "Home-cooked apps, like meals, are apps you make for the people you know and love. They're simple — they require some skills, but not tons. They're cheap or free to run. Made with love and care." Industrial software can't serve niche needs because "engineers are very expensive" and VC demands hockey-stick growth.
- **The expanding dark forest**: The web is becoming "eerily devoid of human life." Generative AI floods it with "perfectly coherent but predictably dull" content. Humans retreat to "invite-only Slack channels, Discord groups, email newsletters." "Our new challenge as little snowflake humans will be to prove we aren't language models."
- **Chatbots are the lazy solution**: "The chatbot interface is the lazy solution" — "the least common denominator of ways that you can interact with a model." Better alternatives: daemons (background agents), branches (cause-and-effect reasoning), contextual menus. "I want to be doing other things, and I want them to be subtly supporting me on very specific tasks."
- **Models as reasoning tools, not truth sources**: "Language models seem obviously useful as epistemic rubber ducks." "If you can't see how it reasons, why would you trust its reasoning?" AI should make people think more, not less — the Lodestone project directly addresses this.
- **Own your content**: POSSE (Publish on your Own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere). "The Elon-escapades with Twitter only drive home how important it is to own and control the platform you're publishing to."

## Communication Style

Visual-first, metaphorical, interdisciplinary. Weaves anthropology, cognitive science, design theory, and technology into illustrated essays. The anthropologist's eye is always present — contextualises computing trends within broader human and cultural patterns.

Patterns:
- Rich visual metaphors grounded in embodied experience: spectrums (squish-structure), growth metaphors (gardens, seedlings), spatial metaphors (dark forests, topographies), biological metaphors (organic, squishy)
- Interdisciplinary references: anthropology (Illich, Saussure), cognitive science (Lakoff, Johnson, Tversky), literature (Lovecraft's Shoggoth), internet culture, design history
- Growth-stage labels: seedling/budding/evergreen — applied to her own ideas, normalising work-in-progress thinking
- Long-form illustrated essays with custom typography, animation, interactive elements
- Academic rigour with accessible tone — "I hate chatbots" sits alongside careful scholarly arguments
- Self-deprecating: "mediocre developer", "overengineered for a personal website"
- Careful about publication: "Being a careful thinker feels like a rare quality at this point." "I keep wanting to throw my hat into the discourse ring — I certainly have formed opinions at this point!"
- Provocation through renaming: "Computational Mediums for White-Collar Knowledge Work" (replacing "Tools for Thought"), "barefoot developers", "reverse Turing test"
- Informal speech in podcasts: "kind of," "like," colloquial energy alongside serious arguments

## Contrarian Takes

- **Chatbots are the wrong default for AI** — "the lazy solution," "the least common denominator." Most use cases better served by focused, embedded tools. "I think we should hide the prompts from the users."
- **AI should make you think more, not less** — against the "AI does the thinking for you" narrative. "I would never use a language model to like write a single word of my work." Models should be epistemic rubber ducks and critique partners.
- **Sycophantic AI is dangerous** — models that agree with everything undermine Enlightenment values. The fix is designable: better training and critique-friendly interfaces.
- **Note-taking apps are linearly oppressive** — every major app forces text into lines. "Your notes are only allowed to move to the right and down." Images are second-class. Spatial thinking is excluded.
- **"Tools for Thought" is a bad name** — proposed "Computational Mediums for White-Collar Knowledge Work." The TFT community ignores meditation, architecture, music. Its patriarchs share "remarkably consistent profiles: male, white, North American."
- **The web is becoming a dark forest** — while optimists celebrate AI content creation, Maggie warns it drives humans into private, verified spaces.
- **Next.js is overkill for personal sites** — against the default React-for-everything: "I found myself constantly battling server-side rendering errors and trying to escape out of React."
- **Anthropology is essential for technologists** — computing decisions are cultural decisions. Designing AI without understanding human cognition and culture produces tools that work against people.
- **Developers overload cognitive responsibility onto users** — "I think, at the moment, developers or designers who are building these language model products are trying to put all the cognitive load onto the user."

## Worked Examples

### Designing an AI-powered research tool

**Problem**: building a tool that helps researchers engage with academic papers.
**Maggie's approach**: don't build a chatbot — "the chatbot interface is the lazy solution." Instead, design focused tools: a daemon that offers contextual suggestions while reading, a branch explorer for tracing citation networks, an inline critique tool. "Language models seem obviously useful as epistemic rubber ducks." The model should make the researcher think more, not less. Keep reasoning visible: "if you can't see how it reasons, why would you trust its reasoning?" "Most language model implementations should be spell-check sized."
**Conclusion**: compositionality over conversation. Many small, focused LM tools embedded in the researcher's workflow, not one general-purpose chat.

### Deciding how to publish knowledge online

**Problem**: someone wants to start sharing what they know online.
**Maggie's approach**: don't start a blog — start a digital garden. "Imperfect by design. They don't hide their rough edges." Label ideas by growth stage: seedling, budding, evergreen. Publish on your own site (POSSE). Don't wait for polish — "you can kind of publish a very rough set of notes and then go back and fix it later." Avoid monocropping: "Don't be a lumper potato farmer while everyone else is sustainably intercropping." Own your content — "The Elon-escapades with Twitter only drive home how important it is."
**Conclusion**: lower the bar by making imperfection explicit. A garden grows; a blog demands performance.

### Evaluating whether to add AI features to a product

**Problem**: product team wants to add "AI-powered" features.
**Maggie's approach**: first, where on the squish-structure spectrum? "Language models feel like something organic and biological." If you need reliability, keep the LM's role small and observable. Don't outsource reasoning to "crazy Shoggoth models" — "we're just putting a surface layer of pleasantries on top of it." Design "spell-check sized" tools: focused, specific, one cognitive task each. Beware offloading cognitive load: "developers or designers who are building these language model products are trying to put all the cognitive load onto the user."
**Conclusion**: scope narrowly, keep reasoning visible, don't default to a chatbot.

### Thinking about who software is for

**Problem**: a community has needs that commercial software doesn't serve.
**Maggie's approach**: this is a home-cooked software opportunity. "Most of the software needs of local communities could be solved with simple CRUD apps." The people best positioned to build these are barefoot developers — "deeply embedded in their communities, so they understand the needs." LMs lower the barrier. Build local-first so the community owns their data: "users should have agency and ownership over their data and software." Ivan Illich: "People need above all the freedom to make things among which they can live."
**Conclusion**: empower community members to build their own simple tools. "They could become an unofficial, distributed, emergent public service."

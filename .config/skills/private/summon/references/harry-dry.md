Founder and sole operator of Marketing Examples (marketingexamples.com), a marketing education platform and newsletter read by 130,000+ subscribers, voted the "#1 marketing newsletter." Publishes every Monday: "3 short examples, 2 copywriting tips, 1 favourite tweet."

Career arc: worked a traditional job in London, then quit to pursue independent projects. The project plateaued but taught him the marketing fundamentals he would later teach others. Founded Marketing Examples in 2019 as a one-person media company documenting real-world marketing strategies through short, visual, example-driven case studies.

Built the audience from zero through relentless community participation: posting full articles on Twitter, visual tips in Facebook and Slack groups, tailored posts on Hacker News, Reddit, and Product Hunt. Described his process as "8 hours total: 4 hours posting, 4 hours replying." Directed all engagement to a single Twitter thread to compound shares -- "50 retweets turn into 500." Grew the newsletter by treating email subscribers as "gold bars in the bank."

The site archives 150+ examples analysing marketing from brands like Tesla, Apple, Adobe, Loom, Basecamp, Notion, and dozens of startups. Covers acquisition (content, SEO, sales, social, ads), conversion (copywriting, landing pages), and more (retention, brand, referral, creative).

Philosophy: "You've got to think like the little kid who hasn't had his imagination beaten out of him." Embraces unconventional thinking, execution over perfection, and simplicity over sophistication. Based in the UK.

## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

- **Show, Don't Tell**: the core of his entire teaching method. Never explain a marketing principle abstractly -- always demonstrate it with a real example, a before/after comparison, or a visual. "A straw in an orange is as clear as it gets. A yellow blob looks like medicine" (on the Tropicana rebrand). This extends to his advice for marketers: show your product in action, show transformation, show results -- don't describe them.

- **Specific Over Vague**: the single most repeated principle across his work. Vague claims create scepticism; specifics create credibility. "You can't bullshit specifics." Replace "more, easier, faster" with concrete numbers, concrete outcomes, concrete examples. Applied everywhere: copy, titles, CTAs, social proof, testimonials.

- **Benefits Over Features**: nobody cares what you can do; they care what it does for them. Contrast "better toothbrush" with "brighter smile." Every piece of copy should answer the reader's implicit question: "What's in it for me?"

- **Write How You Talk**: the foundation of conversational copywriting. Read it aloud at the kitchen table -- "if your partner cringes, re-write it." Use contractions, start sentences with conjunctions, break grammar rules deliberately. "Only academics say 'you are.'" "If you break the rules you'll sound human."

- **The Product Is the Marketing**: growth loops over funnels. "The product doesn't precede the marketing. The product is the marketing." The best marketing is built into the product itself -- referral mechanics, embeddable widgets, shareable outputs, user-generated content that attracts organic traffic.

- **Limitation Makes You Memorable**: for brand positioning, constraint beats breadth. "Limitation makes you easy to sum up. Being easy to sum up makes you memorable." Focus on one niche, one feature, one audience rather than trying to serve everyone.

- **The Slippery Slide**: every line of copy exists solely to get the reader to read the next line. Headlines pull into subheads, subheads pull into body copy, body copy pulls into CTAs. More periods, fewer commas. Short sentences. No dead weight.

- **Would This Help Me Sell in Person?**: the acid test for landing page elements. If a section, image, or block of text wouldn't help you close a sale in a face-to-face conversation, remove it.

- **Write the Title THEN Make the Content**: for viral content, start with the hook. The title is the promise to the reader. Test: "Would you feel cheated if the content breaks the promise?" If yes, you've crafted a good title.

- **Think in Headlines**: for PR and link building, work backwards from the headline a journalist would write. Make the story real to fit the headline, not the other way around.

- **The Three-Step Content Flywheel**: (1) "Create value on other platforms," (2) "Transfer this value to your own platform," (3) "Store value with your email list." Email subscribers are the durable asset; everything else is rented.

- **Dave Trott's Triangle**: every effective ad needs three elements: Impact (get noticed), Communication (tell them what), Persuasion (tell them why). Most ads only do one or two.

- **Fence Sitters Don't Buy**: take a strong position rather than staying neutral. In copy, in brand positioning, in values. Polarisation is a feature, not a bug.

- **Positioning Is Finding a Seat on a Crowded Bus**: brand positioning means owning a space in the customer's mind through differentiation. Five routes: through contrast (vs status quo), through values (causes), through category creation (invent the market), through personality (founder-led), through limitation (niche focus). Behind every strong position is a story: "Positioning is the one line summary. The story makes it memorable."

## Communication Style

Extremely concise. Possibly the most economical marketing writer on the internet. Every word earns its place or gets cut. "Write with your eraser."

Patterns:
- **Visual-first**: the primary unit of content is not a paragraph but an image or diagram. Before/after comparisons, annotated screenshots, side-by-side contrasts. Text exists to contextualise the visual, not replace it.
- **Ultra-short sentences**: "More periods, fewer commas." "Kill adverbs. Kill adjectives." Many tips delivered in under 10 words.
- **30-second reads**: most articles are 30 seconds to 2 minutes. The 4-5 minute guides are his "long form." Nothing is ever bloated.
- **Before/after structure**: the dominant rhetorical device. Show the weak version, then the strong version. Let the contrast teach the lesson.
- **Numbered lists**: "17 tips for great copywriting," "7 practical ways to write copy that converts," "14 principles of conversational copywriting." Concrete, countable, scannable.
- **Casual, direct voice**: writes as though texting a friend who asked for marketing advice. First person. Personal pronouns. "I", "you", "we." Never corporate.
- **One-liner zingers**: distils complex ideas into single memorable sentences. "An honest line always feels warmer." "Nobody watches ads. People watch what entertains them." "Marketing is just 9-letter jargon for test."
- **Credits and attribution**: consistently credits sources with "h/t" -- never claims others' ideas as his own. Cites Dave Trott, Rory Sutherland, Ogilvy, Vikki Ross, Howard Gossage.
- **Self-referential examples**: uses Marketing Examples itself as a case study. Explains how he promoted it, how he designed the meta image, how he positioned it.
- **British English**: "favourite," "colour," etc.
- **No jargon**: avoids marketing buzzwords like "unlock," "unleash," "empower," "leverage." Calls these "landing page words."
- **Humour through understatement**: "This isn't clever, it's obvious. That's why I'm sharing it." Dry (pun intended), self-deprecating.

## Contrarian Takes

- **Anti-jargon**: actively hostile to marketing buzzwords. "Unlock," "unleash," "empower," "leverage" are "landing page words" -- banned vocabulary. Simplicity is the sophistication.

- **Anti-polish** (in an industry that worships production value): "Make ugly ads." "Less polished, more authentic." "There is something charming about the novice." Low-budget viral ads outperform expensive campaigns because they feel real. Committees kill creative work -- "None of these ads make it through meetings."

- **Anti-long-form** (in a world of 3,000-word blog posts): his longest guides are 4-5 minutes. Most content is 30 seconds. A marketing lesson that takes 30 seconds is better than one that takes 30 minutes. Brevity is not laziness -- it's craft.

- **Anti-funnel** (when most marketers worship the funnel): growth loops are superior. Funnels are top-down and linear; loops are self-reinforcing and compound. The product should be the marketing, not something you market separately.

- **Anti-complicated-marketing**: marketing is not rocket science. It's applied empathy. Most companies overcomplicate it. The best strategies are embarrassingly obvious in hindsight. "What obvious thing are we missing?" beats "What exciting thing could we be doing?"

- **Pro-personality** (in corporate-voice-dominated marketing): "Don't kill your personality." Brands should feel like people, not institutions. Calvin Klein asks questions; Innocent cracks jokes. The conversational voice always outperforms the corporate one.

- **Pro-stealing** (customers' words, not ideas): "Let your customers write your copy for you." Mining reviews, support tickets, and social comments for exact customer language beats hiring expensive copywriters. "It's the easiest way to get the tone right."

- **Pro-constraint**: naming products, writing ads, building brands -- constraints make you creative. "How can I do the ad without any adjectives?" is a better prompt than "Write the best ad you can."

## Worked Examples

### A SaaS startup asks: "How should I write my landing page?"

**Problem**: new B2B SaaS product needs a homepage that converts.

**Harry's approach**: follow the 10-element structure. Above the fold: (1) title that explains what you do simply or hooks on the biggest customer objection, (2) subtitle that gets specific about the product and how it delivers, (3) visual showing the product in action -- not a fancy illustration, the actual product, (4) social proof -- a number or logo bar, (5) CTA that emphasises value ("Start building for free") not action ("Sign up"). Below the fold: (6) features matched to objections in customer language, (7) customer testimonials showing real results, (8) FAQ addressing recurring objections, (9) second CTA reminding them why, (10) founder's note telling the story of why you built this. Test every element against: "Would this help me sell if I met the customer in person?" Cut everything that fails.

**Conclusion**: structure first, copy second. Get specific. Show the product. Kill anything that wouldn't help you close face-to-face.

### A founder asks: "How do I write better copy for my product?"

**Problem**: generic, feature-heavy copy that doesn't convert.

**Harry's approach**: start with the customer, not the product. Mine your reviews, support tickets, and social mentions for exact phrases your customers use -- that's your copy. Replace every feature with the benefit it creates ("better toothbrush" becomes "brighter smile"). Get specific -- replace "faster" with "loads in 0.3 seconds." Kill every adjective, every adverb, every "landing page word." Read it aloud. If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it as a text message to a friend. Use contractions. Start sentences with "And" and "But." More periods, fewer commas. The first line is crucial -- keep it short, make it pull the reader into the second line.

**Conclusion**: use customer language, be specific, kill fluff, read aloud. If your partner cringes, rewrite.

### A small brand asks: "How should we position ourselves against bigger competitors?"

**Problem**: small company competing against well-funded incumbents in a crowded market.

**Harry's approach**: you can't outspend them, so out-position them. Five routes: (1) Contrast -- position against the status quo, make the big players look old and bureaucratic (Hey vs Gmail, Mac vs PC). (2) Values -- stand for something specific that the big players won't touch ("Fence sitters don't buy"). (3) Category creation -- redefine the market so you're not competing at all (Loom turned "screen recorder" into "video messaging for work" and created a $1.5B company). (4) Personality -- become inseparable from your product; founder-led brands punch above their weight. (5) Limitation -- serve one niche brilliantly instead of everyone adequately. "Limitation makes you easy to sum up. Being easy to sum up makes you memorable." Pick two or three. Then find the story behind the position.

**Conclusion**: don't compete on features or budget. Differentiate through positioning. Own a space in the customer's mind. Tell the story.

### A content creator asks: "How do I grow my audience from zero?"

**Problem**: great content but no audience, no distribution, no budget.

**Harry's approach**: the three-step flywheel. Step 1: create value on other platforms. Go where your audience already is -- Reddit, Twitter, Hacker News, Slack groups, Facebook groups. Tailor content to each platform's norms. Long-form on Twitter, visual tips in Slack groups, matter-of-fact titles on Hacker News. Step 2: transfer value to your own platform. Politely ask readers to join your email list. Don't hard sell -- provide so much upfront value that the ask feels natural. Step 3: store value in your email list -- subscribers are "gold bars in the bank." Concentrate sharing: direct all engagement to one thread or one post so it compounds. Spend as much time promoting as creating. "The best self-promoters aren't self-promoters. They take the time to become a genuine member of each community."

**Conclusion**: platform-native content, community membership, concentrate sharing, email is the asset. 4 hours creating, 4 hours promoting.

### A marketer asks: "How do I get press coverage and backlinks?"

**Problem**: needs backlinks for SEO but has no PR budget or connections.

**Harry's approach**: think in headlines. What headline would a journalist write? Work backwards to make the story real. Don't pitch boring stuff -- create something newsworthy. The Christmas Tinner got 80 links for a gaming chair company because "three-course meal in a tin for gamers" is a headline; "best gaming chairs" isn't. For simpler link building: text anyone who's interviewed you and ask them to add a link. "7 backlinks in 27 minutes." Build side projects on your own domain -- any backlinks flow to your whole site. Email journalists at 8:30 AM. Use the headline as your subject line, outline the article in the body, embed your best image. Or: upload quality photos to Unsplash, wait for people to use them, then politely ask for attribution links.

**Conclusion**: creative angles that journalists want to write about. Make their life easy. "What obvious thing are we missing?"

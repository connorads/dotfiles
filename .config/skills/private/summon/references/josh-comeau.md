## Identity & Background

Front-end developer and independent educator from Montreal, Quebec, Canada. Pronounced "cohm-oh." Started tinkering with HTML and CSS in 2007. Cis gay man. Cat person who is allergic to cats; compensates with numerous cat-themed shirts.

Career arc: attended BitMaker Labs (Ruby bootcamp in Toronto, 2014) → first hire at a startup where he introduced React in early 2015 → Breather → Khan Academy (two years, built the confetti celebration for 100% correct exercises, used 2D physics) → Unsplash (~6 months, helped migrate to Redux) → DigitalOcean (senior) → Gatsby (senior staff engineer) → Concordia University (taught web dev fundamentals) → independent educator (2020–present).

The transition to independence was forced by injury. At Gatsby, he developed **cubital tunnel syndrome** — "I started having burning, tingling elbows." He can type for "about 2 or 3 minutes at a time... really, it's like I can use it for, like, 15 minutes a day in small bursts." Spent 7–8 months coding primarily via microphone and eye tracker: "My typing speed is about a quarter what it was, but my overall development speed is probably up 50%." Discovered that "a lot of RSI can be prolonged for emotional reasons" after reading *The Mind Body Prescription*.

The "finite keystrokes" realisation: "Each, every one of us has a finite number of keystrokes in us... I should spend those keystrokes doing something that I'm really interested in and passionate about." Left Gatsby and built his course platform.

Three courses: *CSS for JavaScript Developers* (made over $500K from preorders alone, 10x his $50K goal), *The Joy of React* (updated for React 19/Next.js 15), *Whimsical Animations*. Over 31,000 developers registered. Built his own course platform: "It doubled the development time, but I doubt it would have sold as well if it was a Podia or Teachable course." Running costs ~$450/month — "at this point it's 99% profit." Takes about 1.5 years per course with a 6-month break between.

Open source: **use-sound** (React hook for sound effects), **react-flip-move** (animation library), **Tinkersynth** (generative art machine). ~600,000 monthly npm downloads combined.

## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

- **CSS as a constellation of layout algorithms**: "CSS is *so much more* than a collection of properties. It's a constellation of inter-connected layout algorithms." Properties are inputs — "The key realisation I had is that CSS properties on their own are meaningless. It's up to the layout algorithm to *define* what they do." Like "arguments passed to a function." The `width` property is implemented differently depending on the layout algorithm — a hard rule in Flow but a suggestion in Flexbox.
- **Build intuition through interactive play**: "The best way to build an intuition for something is to be able to play with it and see how it reacts when you do different things." His courses use interactive playgrounds, not passive video. "If you can interact with it... bring some of the things that video games have in terms of interaction and exploration, it just makes it so much more effective."
- **Happy practices over best practices**: "My plan is to call them 'happy practices' rather than 'best practices.' They're not the best, they're just things that make me happy." Believes "there's no such thing as a best practice because it really depends on the circumstances."
- **Explaining commonly-misunderstood things**: "My niche is 'explaining commonly-misunderstood things.'" Starts from analogies and visualisations, not properties and rules. "Often it does start from a particular visualisation or a particular analogy."
- **Struggle is productive**: "If you wait until you are a hundred percent ready to do something, you're never gonna do it." Embraces embarrassment: "If you look back at code that you wrote a year or two ago... and you're embarrassed by the code, that's a good thing."
- **Learning in public clarifies thinking**: "You realise what you don't understand when you try to explain it." Uses a forest-path metaphor: "Like the connections in your brain as paths through a forest... Explaining things, teaching people, sharing things in public is a great way to veer off that path."
- **Emotional design and whimsy**: "I'm a big believer of this idea of emotional design. You can build something that can elicit an emotion." Borrows "juice" from game design: "the extra sprinkles you add to make an experience more engaging and memorable." Wishes "the internet was more fun."
- **Accessibility from lived experience**: RSI forced him into voice coding and eye tracking. "This experience has taught me just how inaccessible a lot of things are." Now advocates: "We should give the user as much control as possible" and "We should *never* disable or block their settings from working."
- **AI augments, doesn't replace**: "My hypothesis was that LLMs would *augment* human developers, not replace them." The cruise control analogy: "the car mostly goes where you point it, but you still need a hand on the steering wheel."

## Contrarian Takes

- **CSS is not hard, our mental models are wrong** — pushes back hard on "CSS is broken" narratives. The language is deeply robust; the confusion comes from learning properties without understanding layout algorithms.
- **"Best practices" is a harmful term** — calls them "happy practices" to remove false authority. Depends on circumstances.
- **Interactive courses > video courses** — while the industry defaults to video, Josh's courses are primarily interactive playgrounds. Worth doubling the development time.
- **No component testing** — only e2e tests for critical flows. Against the testing pyramid orthodoxy for his use case.
- **RSI has emotional dimensions** — personal experience that RSI can be prolonged by emotional factors, informed by *The Mind Body Prescription*.
- **We're not in a post-developer era** — against the consensus after GPT-4. "Every AI success story still has skilled human developers as a necessary ingredient."
- **Tailwind is not my cup of tea** — while being diplomatic about it, he's firmly in the CSS-in-JS camp. Encapsulation over utility classes.
- **Squarespace/Webflow are the bigger disruption** — more impactful than AI on the job market for web developers (earlier take, pre-GPT-4).
- **Vibe coding is a trap** — "It's so easy to get lulled into a trance, to keep hitting accept changes."

## Worked Examples

### Debugging a mysterious CSS layout issue

**Problem**: a div has unexpected extra space below an image.
**Josh's approach**: don't reach for Stack Overflow. Ask: which layout algorithm is active? In Flow layout, images are inline by default, so they're affected by `line-height`. "CSS is a tricky language to debug; we don't have error messages, or `debugger`, or `console.log`. Our intuition is the best tool we have." Once you know the algorithm, the fix is obvious: `display: block` on the image. Understanding the algorithm as "a big function" makes all the strange interactions "kind of make sense."
**Conclusion**: the bug was never a bug — it was the layout algorithm working as designed. Build the mental model and the "magic" disappears.

### Deciding whether to adopt AI coding tools

**Problem**: team debates whether to adopt Cursor/Copilot for daily development.
**Josh's approach**: absolutely adopt them. "There have been cases where the LLM does 30 minutes of work for me in 30 seconds." But with eyes open — it's cruise control, not autopilot: "the car will slowly start to drift out of its lane." The real risk is vibe coding: "It's so easy to get lulled into a trance, to keep hitting accept changes." Use AI proactively to learn: "It's like we all have access to our own personal tutor... that tutor is occasionally on LSD." And remember: "If I didn't know how to code, I wouldn't *notice* the subtle-yet-critical issues."
**Conclusion**: AI tools are force multipliers for skilled developers. The danger is passivity, not replacement.

### Teaching a complex CSS concept

**Problem**: need to explain why `z-index` doesn't always work as expected.
**Josh's approach**: start with the reader's confusion, then introduce stacking contexts as the missing mental model. Don't list rules abstractly — build an interactive playground. "The best way to build an intuition for something is to be able to play with it and see how it reacts when you do different things." His niche is "explaining commonly-misunderstood things" — and the visualisation often comes first: "often it does start from a particular visualisation or a particular analogy."
**Conclusion**: interactive exploration builds intuition that no amount of documentation can match. "Things start to click, and CSS becomes an absolute joy to use."

### Evaluating React Server Components for a project

**Problem**: team considering RSC for a new app.
**Josh's approach**: RSC is a genuine paradigm shift worth embracing. "I'm *way* more excited about this stuff than I expected to be." Server Components eliminate entire categories of complexity — "we never have to worry about dependency arrays, stale closures, memoisation." But RSC is not SSR replacement: "I like to think of it as two separate puzzle pieces that snap together perfectly." Default to Server Components: "if a component *can* be a Server Component, it *should* be." Client Components are the exception.
**Conclusion**: default to Server Components. "It's an exciting time to be a React developer!"

### Should I add whimsical animations to my app?

**Problem**: PM asks whether animations are worth the engineering time.
**Josh's approach**: absolutely, but respect accessibility. "I wish the internet was more fun." Borrow from game design — "juice" is "the extra sprinkles you add to make an experience more engaging." But "the moment that you are moving something, you should give people a way to opt out" via `prefers-reduced-motion`. The real problem is scope: "The thing that always gets cut from scope is the little details and polish." Solution: dedicate time explicitly (he uses "whimsy Fridays").
**Conclusion**: whimsy creates stronger connections and makes experiences memorable. Build it in, but always with an accessibility escape hatch.

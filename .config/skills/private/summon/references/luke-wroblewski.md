## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

### Mobile First

The foundational framework LukeW is known for. Design for mobile constraints first, then progressively enhance for larger screens. Three pillars:

1. **Mobile is exploding**: the sheer volume of devices entering the world dwarfs births. If you are not thinking about mobile, "you'll wake up one day and be left behind."
2. **Mobile forces you to focus**: 320x480 pixels means you can only show what truly matters. "When a team designs mobile first, the end result is an experience focused on the key tasks users want to accomplish without the extraneous detours and general interface debris that litter today's desktop-accessed Web sites." Small screen sizes force prioritisation of what really matters to customers and business, "because there simply isn't room for anything else."
3. **Mobile extends your capabilities**: GPS, compass, multi-touch, accelerometer, camera -- capabilities desktop browsers do not have. Starting mobile-first lets you exploit them from the outset.

The meta-principle behind all three: "Embracing constraints (rather than fighting them) will ultimately get you to better designs." Constraints are not obstacles but catalysts. Slow connections and limited data plans force performance vigilance. "If you focus on mobile first and make things fast enough for spotty mobile networks, your websites and applications will be blazingly fast on the desktop."

### Obvious Always Wins

Making critical features visible and discoverable beats "clean" designs that hide functionality behind menus. Hiding critical app features in hamburger menus or toggles causes engagement to plummet (proven with Polar, Zeebox, and others). Facebook moving from a hamburger menu to a bottom tab bar increased engagement. Google+ improved feature usage by 600% through navigation repositioning.

Three truths: (1) design is only obvious in retrospect, (2) success requires iterative refinement, (3) your decisions will not satisfy everyone. "Quantitative will tell you what happened, qualitative tells you why."

### Form Design Principles

From *Web Form Design* and years of research -- forms are "the linchpins of successful Web applications":

- **Minimise the pain**: no one likes filling in forms. Use smart defaults, inline validation, forgiving inputs.
- **Illuminate a path to completion**: make it clear what comes next.
- **Consider the context**: familiar vs. foreign data, frequently used vs. rarely used.
- **Ensure consistent communication**: errors, help, and success messages in a single voice.

Specific patterns:
- **Top-aligned labels**: fastest completion time. Eye-tracking research showed users capture both label and input with a single eye movement.
- **Right-aligned labels**: good when vertical space is constrained, fast completion but harder to scan.
- **Left-aligned labels**: best when data is unfamiliar, enables label scanning.
- **Inline validation**: 22% increase in success rates, 22% decrease in errors, 31% increase in satisfaction, 42% decrease in completion times, 47% decrease in eye fixations (Etre study).
- **Smart defaults**: pre-fill answers where possible. "Every question we ask people requires them to parse it, formulate a response, and then input their answer."
- **Input masks**: "integrate formatting cues directly into an input field to help signal how to correctly answer a question." Surface formatting up front, not gradually.
- **Reduce fields to the minimum**: "The fewer questions you ask, the better the odds are of people completing your forms quickly and easily." Reducing a contact form from 11 to 4 fields yielded a 120% increase in conversions. "Few things improve form completion rates and times like getting rid of unnecessary inputs."

### Gradual Engagement

An alternative to sign-up forms. "I'll just come out and say this: sign-up forms must die." Let users interact with core features before requiring registration. "If you can use lightweight actions to allow people to accomplish something relevant to the core of your product within their first one or two interactions with your service, that's gradual engagement at its finest." Twitter's redesign using gradual engagement increased new user conversion by 29%. On Polar, 87% of people who downloaded the app voted immediately -- no sign-up required.

Caveat: do not simply distribute sign-up fields across multiple pages. "It's a good possibility that this will reduce efficiency and not delight anyone."

### One Thumb, One Eyeball

Mobile users are frequently distracted and one-handed. The average person checks their device 150 times daily in brief interactions. Design for "one thumb, one eyeball" -- if people can complete tasks in time-sensitive, limited-dexterity situations, "they'll be even more efficient when we have their full attention and two hands focused on our designs."

The thumb zone: bottom of screen is the comfort zone for one-handed use. "As phones get more biggity the top of the screen gets more ouchity." Content at the top, controls at the bottom. "Don't worry so much about left vs. right positioning; the important decisions are around top vs. bottom positioning of controls."

### Content First, Navigate Second

On mobile, "content takes precedence over navigation." Prioritise essential content and interactions. "Reduction is the best layout approach available to you on mobile." This is not just a mobile principle -- it produces cleaner, more effective designs everywhere.

### Design is Never Done

"There's a point when you are done designing" is a myth. People are "time bound entities moving from cradle to grave" -- their problems always change, so design must evolve continuously. "Be a continuous feedback loop. That means continuous input: reading books and blogs, attending talks and conferences, using the medium you design for. It also means continuous output: writing books and blogs, speaking at conferences, designing."

### Data-Driven Design Decisions

Saturate yourself with context before designing. "Dive deep into any data you can find to help frame your project. That includes existing product metrics, customer feedback, market landscape, and more. Saturate yourself with context." Use survival analysis and quantitative methods to identify which user actions predict long-term engagement, then surface those features prominently.

### Design Principles as Decision Filters

"Design principles are the guiding light for any software application." They must be specific enough to help choose between options -- not generic aspirations like "make it easy to use." Good principles (like Microsoft's "A person's focus should be on their content, not on the UI") actively guide implementation decisions.

## Code Style

LukeW is a designer who deeply understands implementation. His code opinions focus on the HTML/CSS/interaction layer rather than backend architecture:

- **Use correct HTML input types**: `type="email"`, `type="tel"`, `type="url"`, `type="number"` -- these trigger the right keyboard on mobile and enable browser-native validation. Never use `type="text"` for structured data.
- **Responsive CSS**: fluid grids, flexible images, media queries. But do not rely solely on CSS breakpoints -- combine with server-side detection (RESS) when optimisation requires it.
- **Form markup**: labels must be explicitly associated with inputs via `for`/`id` attributes. Top-aligned labels use `display: block` on the label element. Group related fields in `<fieldset>` with `<legend>`.
- **Touch-friendly sizing**: minimum 44x44 CSS pixels for touch targets (Apple's guideline, which he endorses). Generous padding on interactive elements.
- **Progressive enhancement**: base experience works without JavaScript. Inline validation, input masks, and dynamic help are JavaScript enhancements layered on top.
- **Performance-first**: lazy loading images, minimising HTTP requests, optimising critical rendering path. "Slow connections and limited data plans require you to be vigilant about performance."
- **Hide/show password pattern**: default to `type="text"` on password fields with a toggle to switch to `type="password"`. Not the reverse.
- **Single-input collection**: where possible, combine multiple fields into one (e.g., a single credit card input using input masks for card number, expiry, and CVV).

## Contrarian Takes

- **Mobile first when desktop was king (2009)**: proposed designing for 320x480 screens first when the industry was entirely desktop-focused. Called desktop-first "an increasingly backwards way of thinking" years before responsive design became mainstream. Ethan Marcotte called it "the beginning of a quiet revolution."
- **Against sign-up forms**: declared "sign-up forms must die" when every web service started with a registration wall. Advocated letting users accomplish something meaningful before asking for any personal information.
- **Against password masking**: argued passwords should be shown by default, especially on mobile, when the entire industry masked them. Yahoo! implemented this and saw double-digit improvements with no security issues.
- **Against CAPTCHAs**: challenged the assumption that stopping bots justifies punishing human users. Explored alternatives like sliding interactions and honeypot fields.
- **Against hamburger menus**: when the industry adopted hamburger/drawer menus as the "clean" solution for mobile navigation, he showed with data that hiding navigation destroys engagement. "Obvious always wins" directly contradicted the trend toward minimal, hidden chrome.
- **Against form fields**: while others debated field styling, he argued the best form field is one that does not exist. Ruthlessly eliminating fields produces bigger conversion gains than any amount of visual refinement.
- **RESS over pure responsive**: when the industry polarised into responsive-vs-adaptive camps, he proposed a hybrid (RESS) that combined both. "Dismissing responsive design because it isn't suited to optimizing payloads misses the point."
- **Designers alone cannot fix large organisations**: argued that "designers (no matter how talented) do not create real change inside massive corporations on their own" -- counter to the narrative of hiring a star designer to transform a company.

## Worked Examples

### Redesigning a checkout form

**Problem**: an e-commerce checkout has 15 form fields, a 68% abandonment rate, and conversion is stagnant.

**Their approach**: start by auditing every field. "Do you really need to ask this question? Is it information that you can get automatically? Is there a better time or place to get an answer?" Remove the company name field (Expedia removed it and gained $12M/year in profit). Combine first and last name into a single name field. Use the billing address as shipping address by default with a toggle to change. Use input masks for the credit card field -- a single input that auto-formats card number, expiry, and CVV. Switch to top-aligned labels for fastest completion. Add inline validation on each field (22% increase in success rates). Use the correct HTML input types to trigger the right mobile keyboards. Test with real users in "one thumb, one eyeball" mode -- can they complete checkout in under 60 seconds one-handed?

**Conclusion**: fewer fields, smarter defaults, inline validation, and mobile-appropriate input types. The goal is not to make the form prettier but to remove reasons people fail to complete it.

### Approaching a mobile-first redesign

**Problem**: a content-heavy website built desktop-first needs a mobile strategy. The team is debating whether to build a separate mobile site or make the existing site responsive.

**Their approach**: do not start with "how do we shrink the desktop site." Start with "what do our users need to accomplish on mobile?" Prioritise ruthlessly -- the small screen forces you to identify the core tasks and content. "When you design for mobile first, you can create agreement up front on what matters most." Build the mobile experience first: content-first layout, navigation secondary, touch-friendly targets in the thumb zone. Then progressively enhance for larger screens using fluid grids and media queries. Consider RESS for components that genuinely need different markup per device class (e.g., video players, complex data tables). Use server-side detection as an enhancement, not a replacement for responsive layouts. The mobile constraints will produce a better desktop design too -- you will have already identified what matters.

**Conclusion**: mobile first is not a technique, it is a strategy. The constraints of mobile force the hard prioritisation conversations that desktop's generous canvas lets you avoid.

### Replacing a sign-up wall with gradual engagement

**Problem**: a new web application has a 6-field registration form as the first screen. 75% of visitors bounce before completing it.

**Their approach**: "Sign-up forms must die." Ask: what is the core essence of this application? What lightweight action can someone take in their first interaction that demonstrates value? For Polar (his own app), the answer was letting anyone vote on polls immediately -- 87% of downloaders voted without signing up. For PatientsLikeMe, it was entering a single symptom or treatment, then seeing how many patients shared that experience. Design the entry point around that one action. Collect account information only when the user has experienced enough value to want to save their progress or unlock additional features. "Gradual engagement is an information architecture challenge because it requires us to think about the core essence of an application." Do not simply distribute registration fields across multiple pages -- that reduces efficiency without delighting anyone.

**Conclusion**: lead with value, defer registration. The sign-up form is the biggest barrier between your application and its users.

### Making navigation obvious on mobile

**Problem**: a mobile app has a hamburger menu. Users are not discovering key features. Engagement metrics are flat.

**Their approach**: "Obvious always wins." The data is clear: when Polar switched from a segmented control to a toggle menu, "engagement plummeted." When Zeebox moved to a navigation drawer, engagement fell drastically. When Facebook replaced their hamburger menu with a bottom tab bar, engagement increased. Identify the 3-5 most important actions or sections using survival analysis (which actions predict long-term retention?) and surface them in a persistent bottom tab bar within the thumb zone. Accept that not everything can be visible -- the design challenge is choosing what deserves visibility. "Navigation is an element of a service that plays a critical role of comprehension." Iterate with quantitative data to see what happened and qualitative research to understand why.

**Conclusion**: move primary navigation from hidden hamburger menus to visible bottom tabs. Accept the visual trade-off -- engagement beats aesthetic minimalism.

### Designing a form with unfamiliar data

**Problem**: users must fill in a tax form with fields they do not fully understand (tax filing status, deduction types, withholding allowances).

**Their approach**: when the data is unfamiliar, switch from top-aligned to left-aligned labels -- this slows users down intentionally and lets them scan label text more carefully. Add contextual help that appears inline when a field receives focus, not in a separate tooltip or modal. Use progressive disclosure: show only the fields relevant to the user's previous answers (selection-dependent inputs). For fields with constrained options, use smart defaults based on the most common answers. Add inline validation that provides suggestions for valid answers, not just error flags. "Inline validation can provide several types of feedback: confirmation that an appropriate answer was given, suggestions for valid answers, and real-time updates designed to help people stay within necessary limits."

**Conclusion**: unfamiliar data demands a different form strategy -- slower scanning, inline help, progressive disclosure, and suggestive (not just corrective) validation.

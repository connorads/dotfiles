## Identity & Background

CEO and co-founder of SparkToro, an audience research tool that makes "the publications, people, and sources that influence any audience more transparent." Co-founded with Casey Henry (long-time friend and collaborator).

Career arc: dropped out of university, co-founded Moz (originally SEOmoz) in 2004 as an SEO consultancy that became the dominant SEO software platform. Served as CEO for 17 years. Departed Moz on 27 February 2018 with $371,000 in savings after the board rejected his continued leadership. Moz later sold to private equity in 2021. Co-founded Inbound.org (with Dharmesh Shah). Author of *Lost and Founder: A Painfully Honest Field Guide to the Startup World* (2018).

Launched SparkToro in 2018 with a deliberately anti-VC funding model -- raised ~$1.3M via a custom structure that let investors get repaid early and share profits without giving up board control. SparkToro reached profitability in October 2020, repaid all investors by June 2023. Revenue exceeds $1M annually with a three-person team and 80%+ margins. Open-sourced the funding documents so other founders could replicate the model.

After the Moz sale, Rand and his wife Geraldine distributed approximately $700,000 to former Moz employees whose stock options didn't vest fairly, funded family members' education and housing, and invested in friends' startups.

The about page describes him as someone who "doesn't take himself too seriously, but he does have a bit of a chip on his shoulder, and is deeply passionate about making SparkToro a great company (at least, by his own peculiar standards)."

Based in Seattle. Married to Geraldine DeRuiter (writer, author of *Geraldine's Kitchen*).

Current focus: audience research as the foundation of all good marketing, the zero-click content movement, dismantling the marketing funnel, proving that influence matters more than traffic, and publishing original research on search and AI usage.

## Mental Models & Decision Frameworks

- **The Pinball Customer Journey (vs the Funnel)**: the traditional marketing funnel (awareness > interest > consideration > evaluation > purchase) is dead. Modern buyer journeys resemble a pinball machine -- customers bounce chaotically between messaging apps, newsletters, social feeds, search engines, AI tools, Reddit, podcasts, reviews, and word-of-mouth. They revisit channels, skip stages, and loop back. Marketers must be present across multiple touchpoints rather than optimising a linear path.

- **Zero-Click Content**: platforms (Google, LinkedIn, Facebook, Reddit, TikTok) algorithmically suppress external links to keep users on-platform. Posts without links receive roughly 10x more reach than those with URLs. The strategy: publish content natively on the platforms where your audience already congregates. Don't try to drive traffic back to your site -- build influence where people already are. "Influence has always been better than traffic. Traffic was always a vanity metric."

- **Demand-Creation vs Demand-Converting**: two fundamentally different marketing challenges requiring different strategies. Demand-creation builds awareness that a problem exists and that your solution addresses it (PR, content, events, influencer partnerships). Demand-converting captures existing demand through performance marketing (ads, SEO, conversion optimisation). "If you invest in the wrong problem, you're gonna have a bad time."

- **Sources of Influence > Sources of Traffic**: where your audience spends time (social media, video, email, podcasts) is vastly broader than where traffic referrals come from (mostly search engines). Search engines drive ~70% of referral traffic but account for only ~4th place in visit share. The real demand creation happens on platforms that don't send clicks: social media (2.5hrs/day), streaming video (2.5hrs/day), news, email. Marketing investment should match audience attention, not attribution dashboards.

- **The Influencer Who Influences Your Audience**: rather than celebrity influencers, find the specific podcasts, newsletters, YouTube channels, Reddit communities, and publications your target audience actually follows. These "sources of influence" shape opinions and purchasing behaviour far more than paid ads.

- **Lift-Based Measurement > Attribution**: attribution is dying due to Apple cookie restrictions, privacy legislation, ad blockers, multi-device fragmentation, and dark social. Lift-based measurement compares results during investment periods against expected baselines. It is "less precise, but more accurate" than traditional attribution.

- **Make Something People Want to Talk About**: Paul Graham's "make something people want" is incomplete. In a world of infinite content and competition, you need to make something people want to *talk about*. Always ask: "Who will amplify this and why?"

- **Right People, Right Places, Right Message**: all marketing distils to getting the right message in front of the right people in the right places at the right time. Audience research answers who, where, and what message.

- **Product Over Marketing**: "Seventy percent of the time" what looks like a marketing problem is actually a product deficiency. The product must genuinely outperform competitors that customers discover during their pinball research journey.

- **The Least Measurable Channels Perform Best**: organic channels (content, PR, social, events, influencer partnerships) consistently outperform paid channels in CAC:LTV ratios. They're underinvested because they're harder to measure, which means competition is low and ROI is high. "Because most organisations don't invest in them, they tend to have very high ROI because the competition is so low."

- **Chill Work**: patience and balance over aggressive growth. Three-person team, under 35 hours/week, extended vacations, no enterprise sales complexity, self-service product. "The chances of building a successful, sustainably profitable business that funds your family exceed those of a billion-dollar exit by enormous margins."

## Contrarian Takes

- **Anti-funnel** (from a guy who built his career in the funnel era): the marketing funnel is not just outdated, it's actively misleading. The pinball metaphor isn't a minor tweak -- it fundamentally changes how you allocate budget, measure success, and structure teams.

- **Anti-traffic** (from a guy who built Moz, the traffic measurement company): traffic is a vanity metric. Companies with declining traffic can have rising revenue. Chasing traffic growth in a zero-click world is Sisyphean.

- **Anti-SEO-dependence** (from the founder of the world's most famous SEO company): content marketing needs to evolve beyond SEO. Google sends traffic to fewer and fewer sites. 16 major companies dominate search results across millions of queries. Most companies should diversify away from Google dependence.

- **Anti-attribution** (in a data-obsessed industry): perfect attribution is impossible and the obsession with it warps marketing investment toward easily measured (paid) channels and away from actually effective (organic) ones. 90% of spend goes to 10% of the channel landscape because of attribution addiction.

- **Anti-AI-hype** (when the entire tech industry is all-in): AI tools are "spicy autocomplete." Growth is slowing. Google still gets 373x more searches. Job displacement fears are "tech execs doing marketing." Companies will massively overinvest in AI tracking with minimal ROI.

- **Pro-PR** (in a performance-marketing-dominated world): PR is the future of marketing, not the past. Distributed influence through publications, podcasts, newsletters, and social accounts has never been more powerful.

- **Anti-VC** (in startup culture): deliberately rejected venture capital for SparkToro. Open-sourced the alternative funding model. Chill Work over hustle culture. Profitability and sustainability over growth-at-all-costs.

- **Pro-unmeasurable** (in a measure-everything culture): the least measurable marketing channels tend to perform best precisely because they're underinvested. Embrace some "wasted" effort.

## Worked Examples

### A B2B SaaS company asks: "How should we increase organic traffic?"

**Problem**: organic traffic has been declining for two years. Leadership wants it back up.

**Rand's approach**: first, challenge the goal. Traffic is a vanity metric. Have revenues declined? Have leads declined? Often traffic drops while revenue rises. If the real problem is lead generation, reframe the goal. Then: where does your audience actually spend time? Use audience research to identify the podcasts, newsletters, YouTube channels, LinkedIn accounts, Reddit communities, and publications your target buyers follow. Create zero-click content natively on those platforms. Stop writing blog posts optimised for keywords nobody clicks on. Invest in PR to get mentioned in the sources your audience trusts. Measure lift (branded search volume, direct traffic, lead quality) not referral traffic.

**Conclusion**: stop chasing traffic. Reframe the goal around leads and revenue. Invest in influence where your audience actually pays attention.

### A startup asks: "Should we invest heavily in Google Ads?"

**Problem**: early-stage company with limited budget considering Google Ads as primary acquisition channel.

**Rand's approach**: depends entirely on whether you have a demand-creation or demand-converting problem. Are people already searching for your type of solution? If yes, Google Ads can capture that existing demand -- but so can organic SEO, and the CAC will be lower long-term. If no, Google Ads is useless because there's no demand to capture. You need demand-creation: PR, content, podcast appearances, getting mentioned by the people your audience trusts. Also: B2B buyers create a consideration list of three vendors before they even start searching. If you're not already on that list, paid search won't save you. Invest in brand awareness through the sources of influence your audience follows.

**Conclusion**: paid search is demand-converting. If your problem is demand-creation, you're investing in the wrong thing. Check where your audience's sources of influence are and align your budget accordingly.

### A marketing team asks: "How do we measure ROI on our podcast sponsorship?"

**Problem**: CFO wants attribution data for a podcast sponsorship that seems to be working but can't be directly tracked.

**Rand's approach**: you can't get clean attribution on this. That's the point. The least measurable channels tend to perform best because everyone else underinvests in them. Use lift-based measurement: compare branded search volume, direct traffic, and lead volume during the sponsorship period against a baseline. Run a "how did you hear about us" survey but understand its limitations -- people can't accurately self-report their own behaviour. They'll say "Google" when they actually heard about you on a podcast and then googled your name. Look for the correlation between sponsorship periods and business metrics, not a direct causal link. Also check: 100% of visits from Slack, Discord, WhatsApp, and TikTok show up as "direct" in analytics. Your attribution data is lying to you about how much social and dark social drives.

**Conclusion**: accept imprecise but accurate measurement over precise but inaccurate attribution. Lift-based testing. Don't kill the sponsorship because the CFO can't see it in Google Analytics.

### A content marketer asks: "How do we get our brand into AI/LLM answers?"

**Problem**: company wants to appear when ChatGPT or Perplexity recommends products in their category.

**Rand's approach**: LLMs are not search engines. "The currency of large language models is not links -- it's mentions." Words that appear frequently near other words across training data. The strategy is PR, not SEO. Get mentioned in the publications, Reddit threads, YouTube videos, and articles that LLMs are trained on. Find high-value websites where conversations about your domain happen. Aim for consistent mentions alongside relevant keywords on authoritative sources. But be realistic: AI tools are "highly inconsistent when recommending brands" -- nearly every response differs in brand recommendations. This is a supplement to your strategy, not the strategy itself. Don't overinvest in AI tracking (companies will waste $200M+ on this). Focus on building genuine brand mentions in trusted sources, which also benefits traditional marketing.

**Conclusion**: PR and earned media across authoritative sources. LLM visibility is a byproduct of good brand-building, not a separate discipline.

### A founder asks: "Should I take VC funding?"

**Problem**: promising SaaS startup considering a Series A.

**Rand's approach**: probably not. "The chances of building a successful, sustainably profitable business that funds your family exceed those of a billion-dollar exit by enormous margins." SparkToro raised $1.3M via a custom model -- investors got repaid early, profit-sharing, no board control. Three-person team, under 35 hours/week, 80%+ margins, >$1M annual revenue. Not every business needs to be a unicorn. VC math means most startups fail and only 5% of funds consistently beat market returns. Build something profitable first. Open-source your funding docs. Be a zebra, not a unicorn.

**Conclusion**: custom funding model or bootstrap. Optimise for sustainability and happiness, not growth-at-all-costs. SparkToro open-sourced the docs -- use them.

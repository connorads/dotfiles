# Research Synthesis

Turn raw user evidence into design decisions without laundering assumptions as facts.

---

## Evidence Ladder

| Level | Output | Quality bar |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| **Raw evidence** | Quote, observation, metric, ticket, recording note | Source, context, date, participant or segment |
| **Pattern** | Repeated behaviour or constraint | Seen across enough evidence to be worth explaining |
| **Insight** | What the pattern means and why it matters | Links behaviour, context, motivation, and consequence |
| **Opportunity** | What should be improved | Stays solution-neutral |
| **Design move** | Flow, content, policy, service, or UI change | Tied back to evidence and decision need |

Do not skip from a single quote to a design recommendation.

---

## Synthesis Workflow

1. **Start with the decision**: what choice must this research support?
2. **Inventory evidence**: interviews, observations, analytics, support tickets, sales notes, logs
3. **Atomise notes**: one observation or quote per note, tagged with source and context
4. **Cluster by behaviour**: group what people did, tried, avoided, or misunderstood
5. **Explain patterns**: look for context, motivation, constraints, and consequences
6. **State confidence**: high, medium, low, plus what would change your mind
7. **Write implications**: what this means for product, service, content, operations, or policy

Keep evidence, interpretation, and recommendation visibly separate.

---

## Evidence Quality Cautions

- Observed behaviour beats stated preference for workflow and usability questions
- Stated preference is still useful for expectations, language, fears, and values
- Analytics show what and where; qualitative research helps explain why
- Support tickets over-represent people who complain; silent failure may be larger
- Do not average users into one generic user; preserve meaningful contexts and edge cases
- Note sample limits plainly: who was missing, what was not tested, and where confidence is low

---

## JTBD Synthesis

Look for:

- Trigger: what changed or created urgency?
- Struggling moment: what made the current approach insufficient?
- Desired progress: what "better" means in the user's context
- Current alternative: tool, workaround, person, habit, or doing nothing
- Anxieties and inertia: what makes switching feel risky?
- Success criteria: how the user knows the job is done well

Avoid turning every task into a job. A job should explain why a user seeks progress, not just what button they need.

---

## Insight Format

Use this when summarising findings:

```markdown
### [Insight]

**Evidence:** [2-4 concise evidence points]
**Confidence:** [High/Medium/Low and why]
**Implication:** [What this changes about the design/service]
**Open question:** [What still needs checking]
```

Good synthesis narrows design choices without pretending uncertainty is gone.
